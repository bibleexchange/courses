OBJECTIVES OF DOCTRINE III

 

 

  1. What the objectives are not.
  1. To make personal investigation, study, and contemplation unnecessary for the student.
  2. To exhaust the recorded or unrecorded discussion of this topic.
  3. To make the course unintelligible to students.
  4. To make the unpracticed and superficial student comfortable with elementary presentations as though for children.

 

 

II. What the objectives are.

  1. To aid the student in his personal study, contemplation, and understanding of God with an extended overview.
  2. To make the material and vocabulary of this subject intelligible to beginning students as well as advanced students.
  3. To continue the personal study of the instructor with additions, revisions, enlargements, and modifications from year to year as revelation, discussion, and understanding deem it necessary.

 

 

 

PERSONAL QUALIFICATIONS RECOMMENDED FOR DOCTRINE III[1]

 

 

  1. The ardent love of truth for its own sake.
  2. The supreme and disinterested love of God.
  3. An intense desire to know more of God.
  4. Strong desire to Him known to others.
  5. A willingness to make any personal sacrifice for this end.
  6. A sense of ignorance and dependence upon divine teaching.
  7. A willingness to [apply and] practice as fast as you learn.
  8. A fixed purpose to know and do the whole truth [the will of God].
  9. A state of mind that will not be diverted to make provision for the flesh.
  10. Docility of mind.  [Teachable; obedient; submissive disposition; no desire to rebel against authority, or control.]
  11. Such humility as to be willing to expose your ignorance.
  12. The love of study.
  13. Sound education.
  14. Industrious habits.
  15. Patience and perseverance in investigation.
  16. A mind so balanced as to be duly influenced by evidence.

 

PERSONAL BENEFITS FROM DOCTRINE2

 

  1. A constantly increasing sense of your own ignorance.
  2. The highest advantages for growth in personal holiness.
  3. The habit of rapid, correct, and consecutive thought.
  4. System in thinking and communicating thought.
  5. Facility in preparations for the pulpit.
  6. Exactness in the statement of the doctrines of Christianity.
  7. Facility in proving them.
  8. Consistency of views and statements.
  9. A settled state of mind in regard to religious truth.
  10. Ability to teach the doctrines and duties of religion.

 

 

 

SOME THINGS TO BE AVOIDED2

 

1. Avoid tempting God, by demanding an impossible or unreasonable kind or degree of evidence.

2. Avoid a caviling state of mind.

3. Avoid defending error for the sake of an argument.

4. Avoid committing yourself to an opinion.

5. Avoid calling in question first truths.

6. Avoid attempting to prove them.

7. Avoid begging the question.

8. Avoid impatience at the ignorance or stupidity of your classmates.

9. Avoid an ambition to excel them in study and argument.

10. Avoid a disputatious spirit.

11. Avoid stating one thing and proving another in your skeletons [outlines].

12. Avoid the use of weak and inconclusive arguments.

13. Avoid an involved method of stating your propositions.

14. Avoid stating more than you can prove.

15. Avoid leaving your propositions, until fully supported by evidence or argument.

16. Avoid the accumulation of evidence or argument after your proposition is fully established.

17. Avoid prolixity in the statement of your propositions.

18. Avoid the great error of supposing that truths that are self-evident to some minds are so to all.
