Forgiveness and Honoring God's Anointed Leader

2Sa 1:12 And they mourned, and wept, and fasted until even, for Saul, and for Jonathan his son, and for the people of the LORD, and for the house of Israel; because they were fallen by the sword.

Forgiving people who offend us is very difficult. When a person does evil against us, there is often a surge of anger aroused within us and the thought of seeking revenge crosses our minds. Forgiving an attacker, an enemy is the furthest thing from our minds.

Mat. 5:44 But I say unto you, Love your enemies, bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you, and persecute you.

Mat. 6:12 And forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors.

Luke 6:27 But I say unto you which hear, Love your enemies, do good to them which hate you.

Luke 6:35 But love ye your enemies, and do good, and lend, hoping for nothing again; and your reward shall be great, and ye shall be the children of the Highest: for he is kind unto the unthankful and to the evil.

Romans 12:20 Therefore if thine enemy hunger, feed him; if he thirst, give him drink: for in so doing thou shalt heap coals of fire on his head.

1 Thes. 5:15 See that none render evil for evil unto any man; but ever follow that which is good, both among yourselves, and to all men.

Leviticus 19:18 Thou shalt not avenge, nor bear any grudge against the children of thy people, but thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself: I am the Lord.

Proverbs 24:17 Rejoice not when thine enemy falleth, and let not thine heart be glad when he stumbleth.

Proverbs 25:21-22If thine enemy be hungry, give him bread to eat; and if he be thirsty, give him water to drink: For thou shalt heap coals of fire upon his head, and the Lord shall reward thee.

 

Another difficult situation arises when we are confronted with leaders who make mistakes or who live wicked lives, committing unjust acts. Leaders who are corrupt or who make bad decisions pose a very serious problem for society. Should we show respect or disrespect to such corrupt leaders? Should we honor or dishonor corrupt leaders?

1.  David and his men grieved over the news from the battlefield: a lesson on forgiving one's enemy (v.11-16).

2.  David composed a funeral song for Saul and his son Jonathan: a lesson on honoring God's anointed—His servant (v.17-27).

 

  • However, respecting a leader does not mean that he is never opposed. When a leader commits wickedness or simply makes a wrong decision, he is to be corrected. Neither his mistake nor his wicked behavior is to be condoned. We are never to give license to nor indulge corruption, whether simple mistakes or wicked behavior. But while executing justice, discipline, and correction, we are to continue honoring the position of leadership, the office the leader holds. For most offices of leadership are essential for an orderly, peaceful, just, and productive society.

Eccles. 8:2I counsel thee to keep the king's commandment, and that in regard of the oath of God.

B.  The Ark of God Brought to Jerusalem: A Lesson on Seeking the Lord' Presence, 6:1-2

1  The failed attempt to move the Ark: The importance of obedience and holiness in seeking the Lord's presence (v.1-11

  a.  The mobilizing of 30,000 elite troops to transport the Ark to Jerusalem (v.1-2)

  1)  The Ark of God: v.2-4, 6-7, 12 (v.2a)

  2)  The Ark of the Lord of Hosts: v.9-11, 13, 15-17 (v.2b)

  3)  The Ark where God's presence dwelt between the cherubim (v.2c)

  b.  The disobedience in transporting the Ark: Was to be carried by hand not moved by cart (v.3-5)

(Numbers 4:15; Numbers 7:9)

  1)  David removed the Ark from Abinadab's house (v.3-4)

  a)  Uzzah and Ahio—both Levites—guided the cart (v.3)

  b)  Ahio walked in front (v.4)

  2)  David and the Israelites rejoiced and celebrated (worshipped) as the Ark was being transported (v.5)

  c.  The second act of disobedience in failing to respect God's holiness (v.6-7)

  1)  The oxen stumbled and Uzzah reached out to stabilize the Ark (v.6)

  2)  The Lord's anger and holiness blazed out against Uzzah and struck him dead: Only priests were allowed to touch the holy Ark (v.7)

(Numbers 4:15)

  d.  The third act of disobedience seen in David's anger at God and His judgment of Uzzah: He memorialized the place Perez Uzzah, "Outburst against Uzzah" (v.8)

  e.  The result of disobedience and violation of God's holiness: Fear of God's wrath (v.9-11)

  1)  David was not able to secure the Ark, the symbol of God's presence: Made the decision not to take it to Jerusalem, lest they violate God's holiness and arouse His anger again (v.10)

  2)  David took the Ark to the house of Obed-edom, a Levite (v.11)

(1 Chron. 15:16-18)

  a)  Ark remained for three months (v.11a)

  b)  Obed-edom was blessed by God (v.11b)

2  The successful attempt to move the Ark: The importance of worship in seeking the Lord's presence (v.12-19)

  a.  David was again stirred to transport the Ark to Jerusalem (v.12)

  1)  Stirred by God's blessing on Obed-edom due to the Ark (v.12a)

  2)  Stirred to worship God (v.12b)

  b.  David obeyed the Lord (v.13)

  1)  Had the priest carry the Ark (v.13a)

  2)  Worshipped after moving the Ark six steps (v.13b)

  3)  Offered sacrifices (v.13c)

  c.  David worshipped as the Ark was being transported (v.14-15)

  1)  David wore priestly clothes and danced (v.14)

  2)  The people shouted and blew trumpets (v.15)

  d.  David's wife reacted, rebelled against David's worship (v.16)

  1)  She watched from a window: Did not participate; was an idolater (v.16a)

(1 Samuel 15:23; 1 Samuel 19:13)

  2)  She despised him (v.16b)

  e.  David and the people worshipped after the Ark was set on the tent (v.17-19)

  1)  He made sacrificial offerings (v.17)

  2)  He prayed for the people (v.18)

  3)  He gave a meal to the people (v.19a)

  4)  He dismissed the people and they returned home (v.19)

3  The sarcastic, scornful tongue and spirit of Michal against David: The importance of humility in seeking the Lord's presence (v.20-23)

  a.  David's return home to bless his family (v.20a)

  b.  Michal's cutting remarks: Objected to his wearing priestly, not royal, clothing (v.20b)

  c.  David's humble response (v.21-2)

  1)  He was filled with a spirit of worship for the Lord: To set an example in worship was the very reason the Lord had chosen him to be king over her father Saul and his descendants (v.21)

  2)  He would become a fool—even more humble and undignified—in order to worship the Lord: The people would thereby honor his faith (v.22)

  d.  Michal's chastisement from the Lord: Was stricken to be barren, childless (v.23)
