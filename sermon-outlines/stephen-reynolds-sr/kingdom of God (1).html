The kingdom of God

Ro 14:17 For the kingdom of God is not meat and drink; but righteousness, and peace, and joy in the Holy Ghost.

 

1Co 8:8 But meat commendeth us not to God: for neither, if we eat, are we the better; neither, if we eat not, are we the worse.

 

 

Mt 3:2 And saying, Repent ye: for the kingdom of heaven is at hand.

Mt 4:17 From that time Jesus began to preach, and to say, Repent: for the kingdom of heaven is at hand.

 

  1. Repent;
    1. repentance is a change of mind with regard to sin, especially as committed against God,
    2. which leads a person to hate, confess, and forsake it.

 

The great rite of John was baptism, but the great duty commanded was repentance.

    1. Repentance is more than a sorrow for sin; it is a determination to abandon it and live a new life. It means a change of the will, or heart, new purposes, a determination to leave off sinning. Sorrow is not repentance, but "godly sorrow worketh repentance" (2Co 7:10).

 

 

Repent ye. To repent is to change the will in reference to sin, resolving to sin no more.

 

  For. John sets forth the motive for repentance. Repentance is the duty, and the approach of the kingdom is the motive inciting to it. Only by repentance could the people be prepared for the kingdom. Those who are indifferent to the obligations of an old revelation would be ill-prepared to receive a new one.

 

  1.  Kingdom of heaven;
    1. The Messiah's reign as prophesied by the prophets, or the sway of Christ's gospel and dispensation over the hearts, lives, and destinies of men, both in this world and in the next.
    2. The kingdom of Christ, is the rule of holiness in the inner life.
    3. This kingdom is spoken of in the Scriptures variously, in reference to its several aspects:
      1. first, in this world, as affecting the individual disciple in whose heart it is set up,
      2. as affecting the churches whom it gathers,
      3. and as influencing human society generally, even when not brought into the Christian church; and next, as extending from this world, through the judgment day, when it will be universally acknowledged, into the heavenly world, where it will reach its crowning glory. John the Baptist was its herald. Christ, after his resurrection and just before his ascension, declared his induction into it. Mt 28:18. The millennium and the judgment are stages in its continuous progress; and the consummation of the mediatorial kingdom is described. 1Co 15:24,28. Some texts in which the phrase is used refer mainly to one stage, and others to another, of its onward course. Men must hate and forsake their sins in order to be prepared for the kingdom of God. Pr 28:13.
