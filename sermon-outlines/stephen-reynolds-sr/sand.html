    Pr 27:3 ¶ A stone is heavy, and the sand weighty; but a fool's wrath is heavier than them both.

    Sand weighty.

    The weight of sand

    Not so much intellectual weakness as moral and spiritual weakness, which are the most foolish things that a man can be guilty of.

    The proverb-compares two heavy things, stones and sand, and says that fool's wrath is heavier than them both.

    They are feathers in comparison with the lead-like weight of a fool's wrath.

    What is lighter than a grain of sand?

    What is heavier than a bagful of sand?

    The accumulation of light things is overwhelmingly heavy.

    Is there anything in our lives like that? - overwhelmingly heavy

    1. This reminds us of the supreme importance of small things,  trifles.
      1. The small things make life, and if they are small, then it is small but still important.
      2.  We are poor judges of what is great or small.
        1. We have a very poor estimate of noise, dishonor, and bigness.
        2. We think the quiet things are the small ones and the loud ones are the big ones.
          1. Did you hear that Small sound? Quiet/still
          2. Did you hear that big sound? Loud/
        3. The most trivial actions have a knack of leading on to large results,
          1. beyond what could have been expected.
          2. These trivial actions make character.
          3. Men are not made by crises.
          4. The crises reveal what we have made ourselves by the trifles.
          5. We shape ourselves by the way we do small things.
          6. Keep doing the small things faithfully
    2. The overwhelming weight of small sins.
      1. The accumulated pressure upon a man of a multitude of  trivial faults and transgressions makes  a tremendous  weight upon him.
      2. The words "great" and "small" should not be applied in reference to things about which "right" and "wrong" are the proper words to employ.
      3. Acts make crimes, but motives make sins.
      4. To talk about magnitude, in regard to sins, is irrelevant,  
        1. Small sins, by reason of their number, have a terribly accumulative power; a tremendous capacity for reproduction, destroys so much good.
      5. All our evil doings have a strange attraction with one another.
      6. To go wrong in one direction leads to a whole series of consequential transgressions of one sort or another.
      7. Every sin makes us more accessible to the assaults of every other.
      8.  If we indulge in slight acts of transgression, be sure of this, that we shall pass from them to far greater ones.
      9. An overwhelming weight of guilt results from the accumulation of little sins.

    III. Plain, practical issues of these thoughts.

    1. The absolute necessity for all-round and ever-wakeful watchfulness of ourselves.

    2. This thought may take down our easy and self-complacent estimate of ourselves.

    3. Should we not turn ourselves with lowly hearts to Him who alone can deliver us from the habit and power of these accumulated faults, and who alone can lift the burden of guilt and responsibility from off our shoulders?
