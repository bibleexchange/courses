from BENNY HINN

Say after me, "within me, is a God man."  Now let's say it even better than that, let's say "I am a God man."  When you say I am a Christian, you're saying I am Messhia, in the Hebrew, I am a little Messiah...The new creation is just like God.  May I say it like this, you are a little god on earth running around.

"See, God the Father is a person, God the Son is a person, God the Holy Spirit is a person.  But each one of them is a triune being by himself.  If I can shock you, and maybe I should, there's nine of them.

Somebody is attacking me because of something I am teaching.  Let me tell you something brother, you watch it!  ...Sometimes I wish God would give me a Holy Ghost machine gun, I'll blow your head off!

The Nature of Christ

Jesus Christ knew the only way he would stop Satan is by becoming one in nature with him.  What did you say?  What blasphemy is this?  No, you hear this!  He did not take my sin, he became my sin!  ...He became one with the nature of Satan so all those who have the nature of Satan can partake of the nature of God.

  

As leading televangelists rise to virtually celebrity-like status in our society, we watch in amazement as a host of new and ambitious evangelists strive to rise to stardom each time one of the old guard begins to fall into relative obscurity.  Benny Hinn is one who has clearly risen to the top in Pentecostal and Charismatic circles.  His book Good Morning Holy Spirit sold nearly one million copies, went through numerous printings, and moved Hinn to the top of the Christian Bookseller's "Best Seller List" for an extended period of time.  Hinn's ministry has also shown phenomenal growth.  As the pastor of Orlando Christian Center (OCC), in Orlando Florida, Hinn now boasts of a massive congregation with over 7000 in attendance each Sunday.  In addition, his television program is seen daily on the Trinity Broadcasting Network (TBN), and his traveling crusade ministry plays to a packed house in virtually every arena and convention center where he appears.  There is no doubt that Benny Hinn is an important figure in the American religious scene.  But there is also no doubt that in spite of Benny Hinn's remarkable success, his ministry is very suspect doctrinally.1

           One of the first things that a viewer notices about Hinn's television appearances, are the strange practices he employs in ministry.  In his own unique style, Hinn blows on the eager faithful in his audience, causing them to collapse en masse, in a fashion reminiscent of "the wave," seen at major sporting events.  Of course, it is Hinn's claim that this mass "slaying in the Spirit," is due to the power of the Holy Spirit falling upon the crowd, through the ministry of Hinn himself, as his own "anointing" is imparted to others by means of his breath.  More skeptical viewers may be inclined to attribute this to the condition of Hinn's gastro-intestinal tract.  It is also not uncommon to see Hinn "throw" the Holy Spirit into a crowd, much like a famous athlete is exhorted by his adoring fans to throw a ball into the stands as a souvenir.  Claiming to possess the power of God in his hands, Hinn maintains that his personal "anointing" feels like electricity radiating through his extremities.  Often, Hinn will call those in the audience, who claim that they have been healed, to come up onto the stage and testify about what they feel has happened to them during the emotionally charged crusade.  Hinn may blow on them or "lay hands" upon them individually, slaying them in the spirit.  If they persist in remaining on their feet--in a somewhat embarrassing situation for all parties involved--Hinn "slays" them in a rather aggressive manner, placing both of his hands upon them, then pushing them backwards into the arms of the waiting attendants who catch them.  In the words of one reviewer, Hinn's ministry "bears all the marks of manipulative, staged performances."2

           As is the case with many of these neo-gnostic teachers, Benny Hinn's theological thought is often quite disjointed and shows signs of being developed on the fly.  Like Hinn, key figures in the Word-Faith movement are often theologically untrained and they do not write systematically on any of the various topics which they address.  Instead, they make bold claims to supernatural spiritual authority, enabling them to speak as God's spokesman.  This means that many of the distinctive doctrines taught by Benny Hinn are often expressed in spontaneous and off-the-cuff remarks made in his many public appearances, which eventually find their way into the ministries' video and audio tape catalogues, where these once spontaneous comments now become canonized, and are then disseminated as doctrine to the adoring faithful. 

           Even more troubling, is the apparent willingness with which Hinn has promised to cease teaching questionable doctrines when his orthodoxy is challenged by various theological watchdog groups.  What is particularly problematic about this is that while Hinn humbly admits his shortcomings to his critics and promises to change, he then continues to teach the very same doctrinal errors almost immediately after agreeing to stop doing so.  This gives Hinn's theology a deceptive and evolutionary character, as we will see.  Thus defining Benny Hinn's views on any given point is like trying to pick up mercury with your fingers; it easily slips away if one is not prepared to pick it up with the proper tools.  What Hinn is teaching now, may not be what Hinn has taught in the past, and there is no guarantee that it will be what he will teach in the future.  In fact, the initial outcry regarding his book Good Morning Holy Spirit, is indicative of this, as Hinn's publisher felt compelled to change questionable doctrinal assertions in the book between the seventh and eighth printings without printing a retraction or giving public notice of the changes, something which I consider to be patently dishonest.3

Hinn's Neo-Gnosticism

There is a striking parallel in the teachings of many of America's televangelists with ancient philosophical system of gnosticism, and Benny Hinn is no exception.  Many of his serious doctrinal errors are largely the outworking of his own neo-gnostic world and life view.  The gnostic lineage in Hinn's thought is readily apparent in two ways.  The first is in Hinn's description of created reality, wherein God exists at the top of a ladder of being, closest to pure spirit, with Satan at the bottom, closest to matter.  The second indication of Hinn's gnostic orientation can be found in his exceptional claims to be a gnostikoi or enlightened teacher, who claims a great insight into the divine, beyond that of his followers or other ministers and ministries.

           Hinn's gnostic conception of reality is clearly developed in an early work, War in the Heavenlies, where Hinn lays out an overtly gnostic chain of being as the nature of reality:

There is only one step from the natural world to the invisible world.  Visualize a ladder from God to Satan, the invisible through the visible or earthly into the invisible.  At the top of this line is God.  Under God are angels.  Then a thin dividing line and we find spirit filled people, then average Christians, carnal Christians, and then the division of the Blood line.  Then a moral sinner, average sinner, demon-possessed sinner, a dividing line, then demons and Satan.  Only a thin dividing `line' separates the seen from the unseen, the natural from the spiritual, the earthly from the heavenly.4

It is clear that Hinn has divided reality into various levels, with God at the top of the ladder of being and Satan on the bottom rung.  This means that humanity is caught in a cosmic tug-of-war, stuck between the powers of good and evil.  As you may know, the entire epistle of I John was likely written to combat this same anti-Christian system of thought.  Instead of employing the classical Biblical categories of sin and grace, and giving proper consideration to the Biblical doctrine of the Creator-creature distinction in describing humanities' predicament and ultimate redemption, Hinn recasts the entire Christian faith purely in terms of spiritual warfare along this chain of being, invisible to the naked eye of most everyone except, of course, Benny Hinn, the "enlightened one."        

           The second aspect of this gnostic system is found in Hinn's claim to be an individual with God's extraordinary favor, a gnostikoi, as proved by his professed dramatic encounters with Jesus Christ.  In fact, Hinn's entire ministry is based upon his claims to receive divine and angelic visitations, "revelation knowledge," as well as out-of-body experiences.  Hinn's rise to influence is largely due to the fact that many of his followers believe these sensational claims to be true, and accept them as the basis for Hinn's authority (his "anointing") to minister in the mode in which he does. 

           Hinn's first reported divine visitation was when he was just eleven years old.  At the time, Hinn lived in Jaffa, Israel.  "I really believe it was the moment that God began moving in my life....I saw Jesus walk into my bedroom.  He was wearing a robe that was whiter than white and a deep red mantle was draped over the robe.  I saw his hair.  I looked into his eyes.  I saw the nail prints in his hands."  Hinn concludes, "I saw everything."5  A second visitation came while Benny was in high school and living in Toronto.  One night, Hinn claims "I had an encounter with the Lord.  He came into my room and visited me -- this time in the form of an unforgettable dream."  This dream, in which Hinn recounts how he was delivered from being bound to others shackled as prisoners and who were heading into a deep, black pit, was but a precursor to an even greater experience which followed the next day during a prayer time with Hinn's pesky Christian friends.  "Suddenly I saw Jesus with my own eyes.  It happened in a moment of time.  There he was.  Jesus."6  Those around him could not, of course, see or hear what was going on.  But Benny Hinn, however, knew exactly what had happened to him. 

           It was a trip with a friend to a Kathryn Kuhlman crusade in Pittsburgh, that Benny Hinn claims changed his life.  Experiencing strange "vibrations" while waiting outside for the doors to open to get into the building, Benny recounts uncontrollable "shaking" once the meeting began.  After Kathryn Kuhlman appeared, Hinn experienced a dramatic conversion during the time of worship.  Not quite sure what had happened to him, Hinn tells of how he later prayed, "I've got to have this (what Kathryn Kuhlman had)."  That night Benny had yet another experience, this time, after speaking to the Holy Spirit as a person.  "Then, like a jolt of electricity my body began to vibrate all over -- exactly as it had through the two hours I waited to get into the church."7  This culminated with an eight-hour experience with the Holy Spirit, apparently the next night, in which Benny's room was "full of glory."8  Obviously, Benny Hinn's experiences exceed that of the normal conversion, and the truthfulness of these experiences become the basis for believing the sensational claims that he makes elsewhere.    

           Hinn additionally claims to have seen angelic visitors, and to have heard them conversing with one another.  "I don't know whether you have ever seen angels, but I have....Not only did I see angels, I even heard them speak to one another....I not only saw angels, I heard them talking among themselves."9  Hinn also claims to have had out of body experiences, which he interprets as the same type of event that the Apostle Paul experienced when he was caught up into the third heaven (II Corinthians 12:2).  While sleeping, Hinn describes how he was awakened by a black and evil figure who had entered his room and who was intent on choking him.  "I screamed out, 'Lord help me'....The second I said 'Lord help me' the most unusual thing happened.  In an instant I was out of body.  I assure you I was out of my body."10  Pleading with the reader to believe him, Hinn adds apostolic testimony which supposedly supports his claim.  "[Paul] did not know whether he was in the body or out of it.  Well I knew.  I was out of my body.  I came out of my body in a split moment....I had this experience, please believe me."11 

Don't Touch the Lord's "Anointed"!

Who can argue with a man who experiences direct visitations from Jesus, sees and hears angels, has out of body experiences and claims direct revelations from God?  If Benny Hinn has experienced these things, is he not a spokesman for God?  The answer to this question is a resounding "no" when we discover that Benny Hinn, as a supposed divine spokesman, rejects the Biblical doctrine of the Trinity, the uniqueness of Jesus Christ as God incarnate, among other grievous doctrinal errors.  We would do well to remind ourselves of Paul's warnings about such super apostles, who boast about their great experiences, and their eloquent oratory, but teach false doctrine.

For if someone comes to you and preaches a Jesus other than the Jesus we preached, or if you receive a different spirit from the one you received, or a different gospel from the one you accepted, you put up with it easily enough....For such men are false apostles, deceitful workmen, masquerading as apostles of Christ.  And no wonder, for even Satan himself masquerades as an angel of light.  It is not surprising, then, if his servants masquerade as servants of righteousness.  Their end will be what their actions deserve (II Cor. 11:4, 13-15 NIV).

As with many of these neo-gnostic thinkers, when challenged to defend and debate the doctrinal issues which they have raised, Hinn's response is to do what many gnostics have done in prior ages -- simply raise the charge, "How can you challenge me, since I am God's anointed spokesman?"  This was certainly the case when Hinn responded to his critics, specifically, Mike Horton, the editor of The Agony of Deceit, in a recent televised appearance.

Quit attacking men of God, by name.  Somebody is attacking me because of something I am teaching.  Let me tell you something brother, you watch it!  You know, I don't mind if they attack Benny and the way he is, and the way he walks, but don't attack the anointing on my life.  Don't attack this man of God.  There is a group here in California that thinks they are the judgement seat of Christ.  They judge you for everything you do!  Listen here fella.  Let me tell you something, you are not my judge, Jesus is my judge!  You walk around with your stiff lip and collar on your neck.  Dear God in heaven, I wish I can just...  You know, I have looked for one verse in the Bible, I just can't seem to find it, one verse, that says, 'if you don't like them kill them.'  But there is nowhere in the Bible that says it.  I am sorry I am not exactly the normal kind of guy, you know, I'm from Israel.  Sometimes I wish God would give me a Holy Ghost machine gun, I'll blow your head off! 12

Threatening to blow one's head off with a "Holy Ghost machine gun" is hardly a sign of charity!  The whole point of Horton's book The Agony of Deceit was that it is time for these gnostic teachers to cease from threatening their critics with fire from heaven, and to actually respond to the charges of heresy raised against them.  For if the charges raised against them are true, those such as Benny Hinn who teach such things are outside of the Christian faith and must be treated accordingly.  This is a serious charge to make against someone.  How strong is the case against Hinn's orthodoxy, you may ask?  First, let us examine Hinn's doctrine of the Trinity. 

Tri-Theism

What would your reaction be if you were informed that a major Christian televangelist believed and taught that there are nine separate persons in the Trinity?  "Nine?" you may ask with some dismay.  Well according to Benny Hinn, this is exactly the case.  "See, God the Father is a person, God the Son is a person, God the Holy Spirit is a person.  But each one of them is a triune being by himself.  If I can shock you, and maybe I should, there's nine of them."13  Hinn clearly rejects the orthodox doctrine of the Trinity for a bizarre formulation which is, in effect, tri-theism to the third power!  While tri-theism is, as we have seen, a somewhat popular dogma taught by Bible teachers like Jimmy Swaggart, Hinn's formulation of this takes on a new dimension.  In orthodox Trinitarianism, God is said to be one in substance, and three in person.  Benny Hinn abandons this formula by insisting upon defining the persons in the Godhead in such a way as to destroy the idea of God existing in only one substance.  For Hinn, God is not one in substance at all, but only one in purpose.  Hinn writes of the Godhead, "what is true about one does not necessarily apply to all three.  They are different, even in the way They move and in the way They talk.  We've already discussed the fact that members of the Godhead are distinct persons -- yet they are one."14  But what does Hinn mean by his orthodox sounding assertion that they are one?  "The Trinity, as we will see, is comprised of three distinct and unique entities.  But you need to understand Their Oneness -- Their unity.  It is essential that you recognize that the all-embracing Oneness we are talking about is connected to the work of the Godhead" (Italics in the original).15 

           Thus the oneness found in the Godhead is related only to the work of God, what is spoken of as the economic relationship within the Trinity.  Hinn then goes on to cite I Corinthians 12:5-6 as a definition of the oneness of God.  "There are differences of ministries, but the same Lord.  And there are diversities of activities, but the same God who works in all."  This text is used to set out how the oneness of God is economic (characteristic of tri-theism), rather than ontological (that is, one in substance, as in orthodox Trinitarianism). 

Paul was unfolding the working of the Godhead.  He explained that the Lord Jesus is the administrator, the Father is the operator, and the Holy Ghost is the manifestor....What is the primary work of the Father?  He operates.  And what about the Son?  He administrates the operation of the Father.  And the Holy Ghost manifests the administration of that operation.16 

In other words, according to Hinn, what Paul is telling us is that these three distinct "gods," each with a body, a soul and a spirit, work so well together, and in such harmony that they are one - kind of one big, happy, divine family if you will!

           In order to interpret Paul in this fashion, and to make him out to be a fellow tri-theist, Hinn must reject what orthodox Christians have considered the essential element to make sense of the Biblical data describing God as existing in three distinct persons.  This is the fundamental point that the three persons of the Godhead are one in purpose because they are one in substance.  The Scriptures repeatedly declare that there is only one God, and that each of the three persons of the Trinity mentioned in the Scriptures - the Father, the Son and the Holy Spirit - are each called God.  If these persons are not one in substance, then logically they are three distinct gods, each with their own unique substances.  This reduces Christianity to polytheism.  Benny Hinn, therefore, is not orthodox in his views on the Trinity.  He leaves us with three distinct gods, who are each triune (with body, soul and spirit), and who do not share the same common substance, but are only one in purpose.  Hinn's "god" therefore, is three in substance and nine in person.  This is simply nonsense, for it amounts to tri-theism.

           Benny Hinn's doctrine of the Trinity, therefore, is clearly heretical.  What about Benny Hinn's Christology?  Does Hinn, in fact, actually deny that Jesus Christ came in the flesh?  Based on statements uttered by Hinn himself, this question can only be honestly answered in the affirmative.  What is the evidence for this serious charge, you may ask?  An examination of Benny Hinn's Christology clearly demonstrates the gravity of Hinn's heresy on this point. 

 

The "Little Gods" Doctrine

There can be little doubt that Benny Hinn has taught and endorsed the "little gods" doctrine that has generally become associated with the Word-Faith movement.  This dogma, which teaches that Jesus is but a pattern or a prototype of the Christian believer, and which denies the unique two natures of the incarnate Son of God, has certainly been taught by Hinn in the past.  

Say after me, "within me, is a God man."  Say it again, "within me, is a God man!"  Now let's say it even better than that, let's say "I am a God man."  When you say I am a Christian, you're saying I am Messhia, in the Hebrew, I am a little Messiah walking on the earth in other words.  The new creation is just like God.  May I say it like this, you are a little god on earth running around.17

Notice that in Hinn's mind, Jesus is not the unique incarnate God-man.  In orthodox Christianity, Jesus is seen as one person with two distinct natures - one fully divine nature and one fully human nature, which exist together in one person through the hypostatic union, without division, change, separation or confusion.  Since, in Benny Hinn's scheme, believers are supposedly just like Jesus as to their own new natures by virtue of partaking of the divine nature, there are several logical options that may be exercised to solve the dilemma that orthodox Christian doctrine poses for someone like Hinn.     

           In exercising the first option, Jesus' divine nature must be re-defined so that it is possible for humans to partake of the same divine nature that Jesus had once we become Christian believers.  This means that Jesus' divinity must be transferable, or mutable in some sense.  This option by itself is a rejection of the divinity of Christ, for how is it possible for eternal God to create a second God, since something that is created cannot be divine by definition?  As the Nicene Creed declares, Jesus' divine nature is eternal, being of one substance with the Father.  Historic Christianity has always argued that God is immutable.  That is, God cannot change either in His being, His attributes or His will.  Any change related to God, such as in creation, or in the Incarnation, is change that is external to God Himself, rather than involving an internal change within the essence of the Godhead.  To argue that God's essence changes internally, as Hinn does, (in this and in other places that we will see below), is to deny the concept of God as being free from defect and, therefore, intrinsically perfect.  In Word-Faith theology, God is not perfect as He is.  Rather, He is always in the process of becoming something else, in effect multiplying his essence each time He produces an additional "little god" each time a new believer is deified.  If this option is exercised, God's being itself must be seen to exist in a state of constant change. 

           A second option, is that believers themselves must somehow be raised to the level of the divine in their own "new" natures, so that they can become just like Jesus.  This means that each individual believer must be deified in some sense.  If this option is exercised, one must conclude that every time someone becomes a Christian, their essential human nature is exchanged for a new divine nature, the same as Jesus had.  Thus, Christians cease to be truly human after their conversion, since metaphysically speaking, they exchange their humanity for a new form of divinity. 

           The little gods proponents opt for a position which is a kind of tertium quid, a third option that combines elements of both possibilities expressed above.  Jesus' two unique natures are rejected in this understanding, in that Jesus is not fully God in the classical sense described above, and because man is elevated to the status of a little god who has the same metaphysical nature that Jesus had while he was on earth.  Christ's unique divine nature is destroyed, and man's unique human nature is deified.  In effect, we lose both the true deity of Jesus Christ, and the true humanity of the Christian believer.  Hinn concludes, "we are all little gods running around on the earth."  Since Jesus is the Messiah, and since he is the pattern for us in the new creation, we are all therefore, ourselves "messiahs."  This means that when we are born again, we receive the same kind of divine nature that Jesus had.  This is a complete rejection of the Biblical doctrine of Christ's incarnation.  Ironically, Hinn's doctrine does have some Biblical support, the only problem for Hinn is that it comes from the mouth of the serpent in the garden, who tells Eve, "you will be like God, knowing good and evil (Genesis 3:4)."  

           In Hinn's neo-gnostic Christology, Jesus cannot be the unique Son of God, but is instead presented as a kind of super "spirit-filled man" with a divine nature.  Hinn writes, "On earth, Jesus was nothing less than a total man.  He did not have `revelation knowledge' without the voice of the Spirit.  And he could not move unless the Holy Spirit moved him (italics in the original)."18  According to Hinn, while Jesus was on earth, he was dependent upon revelation knowledge, a concept which is totally without Biblical support.  We know that Jesus' public ministry began after his baptism by John in which the Holy Spirit descended upon Jesus in power, preparing him for public ministry (Matthew 3:13-17).  But to argue, as Hinn does, that Jesus was powerless apart from this event, is an implicit denial of the fact that Jesus was God and man by virtue of his incarnation, not through the descent of the Holy Spirit upon him at his baptism.  Hinn's argument here has much more in common with the ancient heresy of adoptionism, wherein the man Jesus is adopted by God to be the son of God, through the subsequent empowerment by the Holy Spirit.  Hinn makes this point quite clearly: 

Even before Christ faced Golgotha, He offered Himself to the Father through the Holy Spirit....Had he not offered Himself through the Holy Ghost, He would not be accepted in the eyes of God the Father....And let me add this:  Had the Holy Spirit not been with Jesus, He would have sinned.  That's right.  It was the Holy Spirit that was the power that kept him pure.  He was not only sent from heaven, but he was called the Son of man -- as such He was capable of sinning.  The fact that he did not does not mean that He could not (italics in the original).19

Hinn's struggle to deal with Christ's impeccability (i.e., the possibility of whether or not Christ could have sinned), is not unique to Hinn.  Orthodox Christians have long struggled with explaining just how it is that Jesus was really tempted, and yet as God incarnate and without a sinful nature, how could he have actually sinned.  Christians can and do disagree about this point.  But for Hinn, the impeccability of Christ is not really the issue.  The real issue is the fact that Christ's uniqueness does not flow from his incarnation, but from his empowerment by the Holy Spirit.  According to Hinn, Jesus is not unique as to his divine nature as the eternal Son of God, who takes upon himself a true human nature.  Jesus is merely the pattern for all believers who come after him, who can now partake of the same divine nature as Jesus did by virtue of the power of the Holy Spirit.

           What is perhaps the most troubling aspect about this is that Hinn claims that the source of his teaching and views on the subject came to him through revelation knowledge.  In other words, Hinn claims that God himself revealed the little gods doctrine to him.  In a tape series entitled, Our Position in Christ, Hinn declares, "Now are you ready for some real revelation knowledge?  Watch this!  He laid aside his divine form....Why?  So one day I would be clothed with the divine form."20  Are we really to believe that God would reveal to Benny Hinn that Jesus would lay aside the divine form and thereby cease to be God?  And do we really believe that we become a "god" by taking on the divine form ourselves that Jesus kenotically set aside?  This raises the rather interesting question, "Can God reveal obvious heresy?"  But if this was not God speaking to Benny Hinn, who was it?  The choices are few.  Either this did not happen at all and Hinn is not telling the truth (and only God knows the answer to this for sure!), or that whoever, or whatever, revealed this to Benny Hinn was working for the other side!  In any case, the dangers in deriving doctrine in this manner are as obvious as the heretical contents of the supposed revelation itself.

           The little gods doctrine as taught by Hinn and others, is receiving surprising support from some rather interesting quarters these days.  In a recent Mormon apologetic, Are Mormons Christians?, author Stephen E. Robinson appeals to the little gods doctrine as taught by Benny Hinn, Paul Crouch and others, as evidence of the fact that contemporary Mormonism holds to doctrines similar to that taught elsewhere in Christendom.

           Yet even among conservative Protestants the doctrine of deification is still occasionally found.  Paul Crouch of the Trinity Broadcasting Network says:  "I am a little god. I have his name.  I am one with Him.  I'm in covenant relation.  I am a little god.  Critics be gone."  Robert Tilton, a Texas evangelist, says man is "a God kind of creature.  Originally you were designed to be as a god in this world.  Man was designed or created by God to be the god of this world."  Kenneth Copeland, also of Texas, tells his listeners, "You don't have a god in you.  You are one!"21

           It is rather ironic that Mormon apologists are now appealing to gnostic Word-Faith teachers to support their smoke-screen argument that Mormonism is actually orthodox.  In a tragic sense, Word-Faith dogma is known by the company that it keeps.

Gnostic Salvation -- The Exchange of Natures

Benny Hinn's understanding of the doctrine of salvation is also formulated in overtly neo-gnostic categories, which leaves Hinn preaching what the Apostle Paul labeled as another gospel.  As we will see, salvation is not spoken of in orthodox terms, wherein Christ dies for our sins, in that His sacrifice pays for the guilt of our sins which are imputed or credited to Him, so that God can acquit our guilt, and impute (or credit) the righteousness of Christ to us.  According to Hinn, salvation from sin is the result of an exchange of metaphysical natures, wherein we are deified by receiving the divine nature of Jesus.  Consider the following comments from Hinn:

Jesus Christ knew the only way he would stop Satan is by becoming one in nature with him.  What did you say?  What blasphemy is this?  No, you hear this!  He did not take my sin, he became my sin!  Sin is the nature of hell.  Sin is what made Satan.  Jesus said I'll be sin, I'll go to the lowest place.  I'll go to the origin of it!  I won't just take part of it, I'll be the totality of it.  When Jesus became sin, sir, he took it from A-Z, and said "no more."  Think about this.  He became flesh that flesh might become like him.  He became death so dying man can live.  He became sin so sinners can be righteous in him.  He became one with the nature of Satan so all those who have the nature of Satan can partake of the nature of God.22

Hinn rightly acknowledges the blasphemous ring to his words, for quite simply, Benny Hinn openly declares that the Lord Jesus Christ became one with Satan.  Benny is right about one thing.  This doctrine is utter blasphemy!  Again notice the category that is in view.  Jesus "did not take my sin, he became my sin!"  Christ does not have our guilt imputed to him, instead Jesus takes on the nature of Satan so that we can have the nature of God transferred to us.  In the Word-Faith theology, God's saving act in Jesus Christ does not take place upon the cross of Calvary, but in the pit of hell.  In this scheme, Jesus' death on the cross is but the preliminary to the main event, Jesus' descent into hell, where the real action takes place.  For it is in hell ("the lowest place") where Jesus receives the nature of Satan, so we can partake of the nature of God.  According to Hinn, sin is not a condition of the human heart as is taught in Scripture, but is instead the actual partaking of the nature of Satan.  This satanic nature must be exchanged for a new divine nature if we are to be saved.  Thus salvation is not a reckoning of guilt and righteousness between Christ and the believer, but an exchange of natures from Christ to Satan, and from Christ to us.  

           In another instance, Hinn describes the doctrine of justification in categories totally outside the Biblical and Protestant notion of "justification by grace alone through faith alone, on account of Christ alone."  Hinn has stated, "Justification deals with what I am going to do.  Now watch this!  This does not mean that I can go on sinning because justification is dependant on obedience.  If I obey God, I am justified."23  If this is not a doctrine of justification by works, I don't know what is!  There is no mention of faith as the instrument of justification, because in Word-Faith theology, faith is not defined as trust in Christ.  Faith is instead seen as the abracadabra which releases the power of God.  It is important to notice that Hinn completely rejects the Biblical concept of salvation by grace through faith.  Instead, Hinn declares, that he is justified by his obedience, rather than by receiving the saving benefits of Christ through faith alone.             

           In the same television appearance wherein he abandoned the Biblical doctrine of justification, Hinn completely re-worked the Biblical Pauline idea of reconciliation as well:

The blood...has reconciled me back to God....Reconciliation means I am one with God, I am just like him.  It doesn't deal with my problem.  It deals with my likeness.  I am like God right now.  He and I have been reconciled, he's my daddy and I am his son.  He says don't touch my son, devil.  The devil can't come and say to you, well you'll never be like Jesus.  And I'll say "by the blood I am already like Jesus."24

Once again we can see how Hinn twists a Biblical concept into a neo-gnostic pretzel.  In Paul's thinking, reconciliation refers to a restoration of a right (peaceful) relationship between two estranged parties.  In Romans 5, this estrangement is clearly bi-directional, from God to man, and from man to God.  Christ's death removes the source of the enmity between these two parties, which is man's sinful rebellion from God.  But notice carefully how Hinn completely distorts this whole concept.  Hinn argues that reconciliation "doesn't deal with my problem," that is with my sin, but instead "it deals with my likeness."  Hinn is clearly referring to his doctrine of man's fallen nature, which in Word-Faith theology became satanic after the fall of Adam.  In effect, Hinn is saying "I don't need to be restored to a new relationship with God, and have the guilt of my sin removed, I need to become a god just like Jesus!"  Once this has happened, God is now Hinn's daddy and Satan can no longer accuse him, since he's "already like Jesus."  This "exchange of natures" doctrine is completely foreign to Holy Scripture, and as we have seen above is nothing short of blasphemy.

Benny Hinn's Recent "Recantation"

In an important and well publicized interview given for the October 28, 1991 edition of Christianity Today, Benny Hinn frankly admitted the controversial nature of many of his doctrinal assertions.  In fact, in that interview Hinn completely retracted his "nine of them" comments regarding the Trinity, calling his own statements on the subject "stupid."  He very candidly declared that "I really no longer believe the faith message.  I don't think it adds up."25  Hinn even apologized for threatening his critics with a "Holy Ghost machine gun," and presented himself as a humble, and "willing to be corrected" young minister of the gospel.  Distancing himself from his earlier claims to be a prophet, Hinn stated his intention to shun such a label and concluded that "the only revelation knowledge is already in the Bible."26  Many Christian leaders undoubtedly breathed a huge sigh of relief at seeing Hinn's remarks.  "Maybe he is for real," many of them concluded.  Many of those that I talked with who had been following Hinn's ministry for some time with a critical eye to Hinn's orthodoxy, genuinely hoped and prayed that a wayward brother had been restored to the truth.  Others were more hesitant to take Benny Hinn at his word.  If what he said was true, this was blessed news indeed!  However, as one researcher remarked, time would ultimately tell more about Benny's supposed recantation than his words ever would.  In this case, we did not have long to wait.  

Actions Speak Louder Than Words

On October 3, 1991, just two weeks after Hinn had been interviewed by Christianity Today and had given his supposed recantation, Hinn and Trinity Broadcasting Network President Paul Crouch demonstrated the cavalier attitude that those in the Word-Faith movement have toward anyone who expresses concerns about the Trinitarian errors that they espouse.  In this case, what was being discussed on the Praise the Lord show that evening were the accusations made against Benny Hinn for teaching tri-theism.  While Crouch continued his on-going diatribe against those who evaluate the doctrinal content of those regularly featured on TBN by Holy Scripture, Crouch complained that the "heresy hunters" were again after Benny Hinn.  Hinn then took the opportunity given him by Crouch to express his primary purpose in writing Good Morning Holy Spirit, which was "merely to introduce the Holy Spirit to people as a personal being."  This is certainly a noble goal, and one with which Holy Scripture agrees.  But notice carefully, how this noble task quickly becomes a platform to once again reject orthodox Trinitarian theology.  Hinn stated, "My purpose in writing the book, was not to have a theological discussion, or a debate, or a confusing something about the Trinity, and what I think about it.  No, no, my only purpose was to write a book to introduce the average Christian to the Spirit of God as a person."27  Paul Crouch then added,

You know what grieves me most about this all Benny.  What difference does it make?  Either He [the Holy Spirit] does or He doesn't [have a body] and it is not going to affect a person's salvation.  It is not going to affect anything!  The fact that your book is being so mercilessly attacked is the proof to me it's a good book!28

Once Paul Crouch had finished defending Hinn, the discussion immediately moved on to an unashamed exposition of tri-theism from Genesis 18, where the three men in that text were interpreted as Father, Son and Holy Spirit, each with bodies and physical form.29  Keep in mind that these remarks were made just two weeks after Hinn had promised not to teach such things.

           To date, Hinn has not retracted any of the errors in his controversial book Good Morning Holy Spirit (either in the original edition, or in the revised one).  And, as we have seen, Hinn is not orthodox in his views on the Holy Trinity, not only as proved by the doctrine taught in his books, but also by his comments made on TBN within two weeks of supposedly recanting!  Hinn may purport to be introducing the Holy Spirit as a person to the average Christian.  But in actuality he is introducing his readers to the one Paul called "another spirit" (II Corinthians 11:4).  For the spirit to which Hinn introduces us has a body and a soul, which are human, not divine properties, and Hinn's spirit is not of one substance with the Father and the Son.  The tragedy in all of this, is that this does not bother Paul Crouch in the least, for he states that none of the points raised matter, since "it doesn't affect our salvation."  The very fact that Hinn is being questioned at all, is interpreted by Crouch as proof of an attack of Satan upon both TBN and Benny Hinn.  In other words, doctrinal accuracy and orthodoxy do not matter, and any criticism against the Word-Faith teachers is simply Satanic opposition to their message.  The fact that they are challenged at all, is presented as proof that they are right!

           Since actions do speak louder than words, it should be pointed out, that to my knowledge, none of Hinn's heretical teaching tapes have been pulled from Hinn's ministry tape catalogue, and Hinn is still seen daily on national television continuing to receive revelations from God, as multitudes who continue to attend his crusades clamor to be healed.  In addition, Hinn has failed to meet with his critics, as he stated he would do, and in fact, those with whom he promised to meet for reconciliation have instead been met with threats of litigation from Hinn's lawyer when they seek out meetings as Hinn had promised.

           Among Hinn's most ridiculous post-recantation utterances were his comments that Adam could fly and that he could also swim underwater without ever losing his breath.  In an appearance on TBN, in which Hinn even startled Jan Crouch with his assertions that the Hebrew word for dominion indicated that in order for Adam to take dominion over a thing, Adam had to do whatever it was that the thing over which he had taken dominion could do.  "Since Adam had dominion over the birds of the air, he certainly had to fly.  Since Adam was given dominion over the fish of the sea, accordingly, Adam had to be able to swim underwater without losing his breath."30  Hinn claimed, of course, that God had revealed this to him.  My five-year old son was watching the video tape with me and remarked, "Dad that sounds more like Superman than Adam."  Out of the mouths of babes!  Amazingly, it is indeed true that in Hinn's theology, both Adam and Jesus have more in common with fictional super-heroes than they do with their Biblical counterparts.  

           All of this has tragic consequences, for what Crouch and Hinn ignore is the fact that we cannot be saved by Benny Hinn's other Jesus, his other spirit or his other gospel, since the three gods given us by Hinn are clearly not the Triune God found in Holy Scripture.  Despite Paul Crouch's objections to the contrary, this is not a game that theologians play to give them something to do.  This is about the gospel and "contending for the faith, once for all delivered to the saints" (Jude 3).  While Paul Crouch may not feel that this is important, our eternal destiny rides on our orthodoxy on this exact point!  In his first epistle, John warned Christians to "test the spirits to see whether they are from God, because many false prophets have gone out into the world" (I John 4:1).  But how are we to do this?  John tells us very clearly: 

This is how you can recognize the Spirit of God:  Every spirit that acknowledges that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is from God, but every spirit that does not acknowledge Jesus is not from God.  This is the spirit of the antichrist, which you have heard is coming and even now is already in the world (I John 4:2-3 NIV).

Benny Hinn, who repeatedly denies that Jesus Christ is God in human flesh, is therefore, an antichrist.  There is absolutely no evidence that Benny Hinn has made good on his claims that were made to Christianity Today.  Within two weeks of giving his interview, Hinn was back to his same old ways and has shown absolutely no signs of demonstrable change in doctrine or attitude from that point on.

The Dilemma Posed by Benny Hinn

How can someone who has sold so many books and who has become so influential in American evangelicalism be so deeply involved in grievous doctrinal error?  How can we respond to someone who has a large and growing ministry unless the size of our ministry matches Hinn's?  Do we dare touch God's anointed?  There are several answers to this.  First, the Scriptures never exhort us to count noses as the means to find out who has God's approval.  Instead, the Bible demands that we evaluate the doctrine of each and every teacher by the doctrine already contained in the Word of God.  When Paul wrote his letter to the Galatians, he was opposing a group known as the Judaizers, who boasted about their large number of followers (Galatians 6:13).  Like Benny Hinn, the Judaizers were leading many good-intentioned Christians astray.  These Judaizers were emphatically denounced by the Apostle Paul himself, for departing from the gospel which he had given to them.

I am astonished that you are so quickly deserting the one who called you by the grace of Christ and are turning to a different gospel -- which is really no gospel at all.  Evidently some people are throwing you into confusion and are trying to pervert the gospel of Christ.  But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach a gospel other than the one we preached to you, let him be eternally condemned!  As we have already said, so now I say again:  If anybody is preaching to you a gospel other than what you accepted, let him be eternally condemned (Galatians 1:6-9 NIV)!

I find it rather interesting that Paul identifies angels as a possible source of the problem!  Perhaps Benny Hinn's angelic visitors have some bearing upon the false gospel that he preaches.  In any case, Paul's stern warnings most certainly apply to someone like Hinn, who re-defines the Trinity, rejects the historic understanding of the deity of Christ, and teaches a doctrine of salvation which is clearly another Gospel.

           A second factor which must be considered are the warnings given us in Holy Scripture about the signs and conditions during the last days.  In II Thessalonians, Paul warns of a great apostasy.  "The coming of the lawless one," who many believe will be the final culmination of the spirit of antichrist whom John describes as anyone who denies that Jesus is God in human flesh (I John 4:1-3), "will be in accordance with the work of Satan displayed in all kinds of counterfeit miracles" (II Thessalonians 2:9).  Paul plainly warns that immediately before the end, counterfeit miracles will be in open display.  These false miracles and signs that will "deceive those who are perishing.  They perish because they refused to love the truth and so be saved" (v. 10).  Paul concludes this warning with one of the most frightening passages in Holy Scripture: "For this reason God sends them a powerful delusion so that they will believe the lie and so that all will be condemned who have not believed the truth but have delighted in wickedness (v. 11-12)."  Those who perish are those who do not love the truth, but instead love the perversion, which is based on false signs and wonders.  These are frightening words and must be heeded.  It is my prayer that God grant Benny Hinn repentance from his rejection of the truth before it is too late.
