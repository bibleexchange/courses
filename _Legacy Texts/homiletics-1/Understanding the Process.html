Understanding the Process

 

To appreciate the process of teamwork, look at the figure below.  Assuming it is symmetrical (i.e., square), take a few minutes to write down exactly how many squares there are in the figure.  (This exercise serves as a reference point throughout the book.)   

 

How many squares did you come up with?  Would you believe that a group of twenty people will respond with as many as twelve different answers?  Naturally, this leaves a lot of room for misunderstanding and conflict.  If a group of people can look at the same thing and arrive at totally different conclusions, imagine the impact this has on team relationships—especially if they all think they are right.  Did you count sixteen?  Seventeen?  Twenty-one?  Twenty- two?  Twenty-four?  Twenty-five?  Twenty-six?  Twenty- seven?  Twenty-eight?  Twenty-nine?  These are all common answers.  If your answer is any one of them, however, keep looking.  The correct answer is thirty (sixteen one- by-ones, nine two-by-twos, four three-by-threes, one four- by-four)

 

 

 

The message in this exercise is twofold.  First, people can look at the same thing and see something quite different.  Second, people often come up short because they jump to conclusions and don’t probe far enough.  They become impatient with the process and look instead for a quick solution.  When this happens, people tend to form opinions and base decisions on assumption rather than on fact.  Both of these tendencies create unnecessary conflict among team members, especially when the problem is complex and people approach it from a “me-opic” perspective

 

Now replace the squares with the subject of teamwork.  How do you define teamwork?  Do you think that your definition is consistent with your co-workers’?  What are the implications if it isn’t?  Could people be thinking they are performing as team players when in fact they are not?  Could you be one of them?

 

 

Defining Teamwork

 

The first step in cultivating teamwork is defining what teamwork means.  Like Leonard, Shirley, and Fred, many people interpret teamwork differently.  In other words, they see differing numbers of squares.  Without an accurate definition of teamwork, it will be difficult for people like Leonard, Shirley, and Fred to actually achieve it.  Here is a simple definition of teamwork:  

 

Teamwork is a cooperative effort in which individual interests are subordinated to the interests and needs of the team

 

 Teamwork means “giving up some of the me for the we.” In other words, teamwork is a process stemming from a “we-opic,” or “we-focused,” mindset.
