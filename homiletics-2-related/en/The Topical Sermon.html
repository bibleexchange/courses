The Topical Sermon

 

 

  1. Definition of the Topical Sermon
  1. Founded on the theme or topic of the text,
  2. Rather than on the words of the text.
  3. I.e., the theme is the most prominent in a topical sermon.

 

  1. Advantages of the Topical Sermon
  1. It allows for rhetorical perfection.  (If the aim of preaching be to make the sermon a work of art, let it be treated topically.
  2. It allows for thorough examination of the theme.  (A single text rarely does this.)
  3. It trains the mind to breadth of view.  (The spirit of the words is what we should aim to bring into the light, and not just the bare words.)

 

  1. Treatment of the Topical Sermon
  1. Its style.
  1. RHETORICAL  (See and study English II notes)
  2. ILLUSTRATIVE  (Opportunity for illustrations, “stories”.)
  3. CLIMACTIC  (Rises to successive climaxes reaching to a well-defined conclusion.)
  4. PERSUASIVE  (Ends with recapitulation, application, and earnestness.)
  1. The element of argument should be found in every sermon.  Action should be our aim.  The proposition must be proved to get proper action.

  2. Without plain, direct application the preacher does little more than fire blank cartridges; he invites us to a field day rather than challenges us to a conflict.—T. Harwood Pattison

    Plain, direct application should be found in every sermon.  No sermon should lack application.  No congregation should be deprived of application.

 

 

 

  1. Its arrangement.
  1. PROGRESSIVE  (The points are indicated, reached, and reviewed.)
  2. EXHAUSTIVE  (Every facet of the “gem” is held to the light.)
