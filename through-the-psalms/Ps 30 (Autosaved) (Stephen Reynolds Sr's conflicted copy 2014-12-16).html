A Warning and Admonition to a Defiant Age, 30:10-33

Introduction: Evil Prevails when good men do nothing

 “All that is necessary for the triumph of evil is that good men do nothing.”

This phenomenal statement was made by Edmund Burke, an eighteenth century Irish politician, philosopher, and statesman. As a member of the House of Commons in the British Parliament, he was a great supporter of the American Revolution. In addition to being relevant for every era, this brief statement has the power to convict, convert, or condemn its every hearer, reader, and even speaker.

Agur was a good and godly man who saw the evil in his generation and decided he had to do something: he forcefully spoke out against the wickedness of his day.

Agur was an oracle—his message was from God—He began with a testimony about how he came to know God through God’s creation and His Word (1-10).

Agur conveys some desperately-needed wisdom to his defiant age.

His teaching is rich with illustrations from nature, and it features a poetic method common in ancient Eastern literature that used numbers and lists. His numerical proverbs are in the fashion of Solomon’s list of the sins God hates (6:16-19).

This is, A Warning and Admonition to a Defiant Age, 30:10-33.

1. (30:10-14) Agur’s warning to a defiant age.

Agur surveyed the generation in which he lived and observed five groups of people whose actions were especially vile.

These sinners lived in open rebellion to God’s law. They defied God and defiled the people around them. God stirred Agur’s heart to speak out against these sins that so characterized his day.

a. Never slander (seek advantage over) a worker [servant] to his employer: You will be cursed (v. 10).

Agur witnessed wicked people rising up against innocent servants and offered this warning: never speak ill of a worker to his or her employer.

Accuse (lashan) literally means to use or wag the tongue. It always has a negative implication and destructive intent, and it is accurately translated as slander (Ps.101:5).

Ps 101:5 Whoso privily slandereth his neighbour, him will I cut off: him that hath an high look and a proud heart will not I suffer.

The motive behind it is to gain an advantage over or to do harm to the one accused. Accusers are meddlers; they interfere in matters that are not their concern

We need to appreciate men like Agur that will speak up and help this generation wake up to righteousness.

b. Guard against disrespect of parents (v. 11)

Agur observed sons and daughters who were rebellious and disrespectful of their parents. Cursing one’s parents is not only a major transgression against God’s commandments (Ex.20:17) but also a sin against all natural instincts. Those who despise their parents are especially corrupt and are held in contempt by all decent people. The law prescribed the death penalty for rebellious children (Ex.21:15; Pr.20:20).

c. Guard against self-righteousness (v. 12).

Pr 30:12 There is a generation that are pure in their own eyes, and yet is not washed from their filthiness.

People live in open immorality, indulging in lustful pleasures, drunkenness, wild parties, and other excesses while boasting they were doing no wrong. They had no shame and boldly flaunted their perverse, wicked ways. They are “devoid of moral standards and scornful of traditional and spiritual values.” In their eyes, they are pure—clean from all sin                                                 Pr 16:2 All the ways of a man are clean in his own eyes; but the LORD weigheth the spirits.

 They believed that their participation in religious rituals made them clean. Such self-righteous people usually twist the Word of God to deny the sinfulness of their behavior, and they persecute those who do not approve of their lifestyles. Agur’s description of them is graphically crude: they think they are clean when they are actually covered in their own filthiness (tsow’ah)— their own excrement.                                                                                                              Pr 30:12 There is a generation that are pure in their own eyes, and yet is not washed from their filthiness.

d. Guard against haughtiness—pride—self-exaltation (v. 13).

Pr 30:13 There is a generation, O how lofty are their eyes! and their eyelids are lifted up.

Proud people are described as having haughty eyes (Ps.101:5, 131:1). They arrogantly look down on others and on God’s law. They exalt themselves over God and serve themselves and their desires rather than God (Ro. 1:24-26). Raised or lifted up eyelids describes the proud look so bitterly despised by God                                                                                                Pr 6:17 A proud look, a lying tongue, and hands that shed innocent blood,

2. (30:15-33) Agur’s admonition to a defiant age.

Agur’s message was with proverbs. God had revealed himself to Agur through creation, and many of these lessons are drawn from his observation of nature                                                                                                                              Pr 30:4 Who hath ascended up into heaven, or descended? who hath gathered the wind in his fists? who hath bound the waters in a garment? who hath established all the ends of the earth? what is his name, and what is his son's name, if thou canst tell?

a. Guard against greed—never having enough (15-16).

Agur was fully aware of the ugliness of greed. Remember, he had even prayed that God would give him only as much as he needed for his own sustenance (8-9). He prized contentment and he cautioned against covetousness.

Agur compared greedy people to leeches. Leeches attach themselves to living creatures and actually live off their blood. The leech’s two daughters refers to that which it draws blood. They continuously cry, “Give! Give!” and are never satisfied. This is the selfish, detestable trait of the greedy; they never have enough. Agur gives four additional illustrations that describe the greedy:

  1. The grave (sheol)—the place that holds the dead (27:20; Hab. 2:5). Death never retreats; it never claims its last life. It continues to crave corpses and never rejects any body. Likewise, the greedy are always craving far more, never feeling satisfied or fulfilled.
  2. The barren womb. The pain a woman suffers who desires children but cannot bear them is endless and tormenting. She feels empty and hurt. Consider the bitter desperation of Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah (Ge.16:1-2; 30:1; 1 Sa.1:5-11). Nothing can satisfy the childless woman’s craving for a baby to hold in her arms. So it is with the greedy: a sense of desire always gnaws at their hearts and nothing ever satisfies their craving.
  3. The earth that is never fully watered. Agur lived in a dry region that suffered from frequent droughts. The land in Palestine and the surrounding areas was always thirsty. It desperately absorbed every drop of rain that fell. Just like the dry, unwatered earth, the greedy thirst for more and more, never getting enough.
  4. The raging fire that burns out of control in the dry, brittle wilderness. It never stops, but continues to
    advance and spread its flames without boundaries. Greed can eat at the human soul until its flames are unquench-able—totally out of control.

The mid-size raven pecks a sightless eye out of its socket, and the larger, stronger vulture (eagle) plucks it away and devours it. In Jewish society, an unburied corpse was a great disgrace and a symbol of God’s merciless judgment (De.28:26; Je.16:4; Re.19:17-21).

c. Consider four amazing things in nature (vv. 18-19).

God is immeasurably powerful and the Author of many creative wonders. As Agur observed and meditated on the world around him, he cited four natural acts that were marvelous beyond his understanding. Note that these proverbs are united by the word way (derek). Throughout Proverbs, this word is commonly used to refer to the paths of the righteous and wicked. Agur marveled at the way these four marvels moved and acted.

1) The soaring of the eagle through the air (v. 19a). Eagle (nesher) is a Hebrew word for large birds in general. The same word is previously used in Pr 30:17 The eye that mocketh at his father, and despiseth to obey his mother, the ravens of the valley shall pick it out, and the young eagles shall eat it.

The context in which it is used determines which species is in view. Eagles are known for their strength, majesty, and swiftness. Lesser birds, such as the raven and vulture, were typified as dirty scavengers.

2) The slithering of the snake on a rock (v. 19b). The snake is in contrast to the eagle. The eagle’s strong wings lift it to great heights and catch the wind as it looks down on the earth. The snake, however, cannot move itself above the earth. It looks up at the rest of the world. It has no limbs whatsoever, but knows how to propel itself along. One travels the sky; the other crawls the earth. One glides on the wind; the other slithers on a rock.

3) The sailing of a ship upon the open sea (v. 19c). Who can look upon the great oceans without catching a glimpse of the glory and power of God?  Agur observed ships sailing through the waves, he marveled at the wisdom and goodness of God. God has given humans the ability to design large vessels that will ride on the mighty waters without sinking. The sailor launches into the deep and with no land in sight he navigates the proper course. How? By the natural signs within the world, signs that God has placed in the sky above and the earth below.

4) The intimacy of a man with a woman (v. 19d). a beautiful, holy gift from God to a husband and wife. Maid or maiden (‘almah, also translated virgin) suggests that Agur is referring to the first time a young husband and wife enjoy the full expression of their love.

How tragic sin has so defiled this pure, beautiful gift.

Agur beheld these amazing movements, he saw a glorious God of great variety, design, purpose, beauty, and wisdom. But this is not Agur’s main point.

Do they make us stop and stare?

f. Learn that wisdom leads to success: Learn from four small but very wise creatures (24-28).

Agur uses four creatures to illustrate the practical wisdom that leads to success in life and business.

Large physical stature, strength, and resources are not required for great achievements.

The small and weak can be successful when they exercise great diligence, savvy, and skill.

God illustrates this principle through these four amazing
creatures:

1) Tiny, industrious ants (v. 25). Ants survive the winter because they have the foresight and self-discipline to work tirelessly and store their food in the summer, when it is available (6:6-8). The secret to their strength is their unity: they are a people (‘am)—a tribe, a collective unit. They work together as one to accomplish what they are too small to do individually. If we wish to be successful in life, we, like the ant, must have foresight (plan ahead), be disciplined, unified (work with others), and work tirelessly to accomplish our tasks.

2) The coney or rock badger (v. 26). it hides itself in places where it is safe from danger. It is the common prey of larger animals but has the instinctive sense to protect itself by burrowing into the crevices of rocks, where it is beyond the reach of its enemies . We, too, must be wise in protecting ourselves throughout life, protecting ourselves from all who would harm us physically or spiritually.

Ps 104:18 The high hills are a refuge for the wild goats; and the rocks for the conies.

3) An army of locusts (v. 27). Like the ant, they also find that their strength is their unity and their great organization. They have no recognized leader, yet they are massive and mighty:

Pr 30:27 The locusts have no king, yet go they forth all of them by bands;

As an army, they march in a regular order, camp in the field at night, and in the morning rise with the sun, dry their wings, and fly in the direction of the wind. They number in the billions, covering an area up to ten miles in length and five miles wide, and have been known to fly seventeen hours at a time, covering over 1500 miles. Their vast number can blot out the sun bringing a temporary darkness over the earth. Nothing stops them - not ditch, fire, wall, door, or window. Their appetite is never satisfied; they devour all the vegetation in their path.

God used locusts as a plague to persuade Pharaoh to release the Israelites from the bondage of slavery (Ex.10:12-20) and in His judgment against Judah (Joel 1-2). Locusts seem unstoppable because millions work together as one in an orderly fashion. To be successful in life, we must be wise enough to work with others in harmony and to organize ourselves to achieve success.

4) The elusive, aggressive spider/lizard (v. 28).

This creature is not the spider as we know it (KJV)

A tiny reptile which can easily be caught in the hand. It was a common creature (house lizard) throughout Palestine, even in the palaces of kings.

  • active not passive
  • bold not timid
  • aggressive not hesitant
  • persistent not easily distracted or detoured
  • determined and unyielding not easily discouraged

We human beings need to adopt the same traits as this lizard to be successful in life!

g. Learn to be stately (dignified) and courageous: Learn from four royal creatures (vv. 29-31).

Three animals and a king are recognized by Agur as being stately in their stride, dignified in their demeanor, majestic in their march, and courageous as they command and conquer.

They illustrate believers who must be always ready to stand their ground against the onslaught of enemies who will attack their faith.

1) The fearless lion (v. 30). Who will dispute the lion’s title as king of the beasts? His very presence strikes terror in the hearts of animals much larger and swifter than he. His roar proclaims that he fears no enemy and it causes all others to flee. He is tenacious and backs down at no threat. He is unchallenged in the jungle because he is invincible in his mind and spirit.

2) The greyhound or strutting rooster (the animal with girded loins) (v. 31a). This stately creature is not specifically named in the Hebrew text, which is literally translated “girded of loins,” or “loins tightly girded.” The KJV and NKJV identify this animal as the greyhound, the swift racer and hunter with large, muscular flanks. Most newer translations render it as the strutting rooster, with its colorful, meaty thighs. The RV considered it to be the
warhorse, whose bulging, robust haunches were decorated for battle.

All three provide an image of the ready king whose loins were girded for battle.

3) The he-goat (v. 31b). The male goat butts his way through life. He marches at the head of the flock and clears the way for the goats that follow him. His horns and his confidence stand to challenge all who would consider attacking one of his flock.

4) The dauntless king (v. 3b). In ancient history kings often led their armies into battle. They did not command their troops from afar; they were valiant warriors who led the charge.

Like all three of the animals and the king mentioned above, we must live fearlessly and do all things well. Our behavior must be stately, majestic, and courageous.

God has called all believers—all who have truly trusted Christ—to be “a royal priesthood” (1 Pe.2:9). We must stand before God as royalty (as kings) and as priests. As royal kings and priests, we must access into God’s presence any time and any place. Therefore, as believers, we must be like the kings of old: dauntless and courageous in leading God’s people to stand against the enemy.

h. Do not exalt yourself nor plot evil (32-33).

Agur concludes his message with a warning against committing the foolish sins that cause strife.

  1. The first is pride—self-exaltation (32a). An arrogant, high opinion of self prompts us to have vain, misdirected thoughts.
  2. Pride causes us to be overly-sensitive, easily-offended, and demanding of our rights.
  3. This leads us to devise ungodly and selfish plans to get our own way or to obtain our own satisfaction (32b).

Agur’s urgent direct advice:

submit yourself to the authority of God;

suppress your selfish emotions;

stop your foolish plans.

He counsels the proud to lay their hands over their mouths. Covering the mouth with the hand was a sign of humility and repentance:

Pr 30:32 If thou hast done foolishly in lifting up thyself, or if thou hast thought evil, lay thine hand upon thy mouth.

“Behold, I am vile; what shall I answer thee? I will lay mine hand upon my mouth” (Jb.40:4).

“The nations shall see and be confounded at all their might: they shall lay their hand upon their mouth, their ears shall be deaf” (Mic.7:16).

Sins of pride provoke others to anger, and anger produces strife (v. 33).

Pr 30:33 Surely the churning of milk bringeth forth butter, and the wringing of the nose bringeth forth blood: so the forcing of wrath bringeth forth strife.

Certain actions have inevitable results. Two examples are given: churning milk produces butter, and wringing or twisting the nose causes it to bleed. When anger is provoked, contention and dissension are sure to follow. These can be avoided by being humble, selfless, and submitting to the Lord.
