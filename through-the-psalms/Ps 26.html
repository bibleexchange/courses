 2 Examine me, O LORD, and prove me; try my reins and my heart.

 3 For thy lovingkindness is before mine eyes: and I have walked in thy truth.

 4 I have not sat with vain persons, neither will I go in with dissemblers.

 5 I have hated the congregation of evil doers; and will not sit with the wicked.

 6 I will wash mine hands in innocency: so will I compass thine altar, O LORD:

 7 That I may publish with the voice of thanksgiving, and tell of all thy wondrous works.

 8 LORD, I have loved the habitation of thy house, and the place where thine honour dwelleth.

 9 Gather not my soul with sinners, nor my life with bloody men:

 10 In whose hands is mischief, and their right hand is full of bribes.

 11 But as for me, I will walk in mine integrity: redeem me, and be merciful unto me.

 12 My foot standeth in an even place: in the congregations will I bless the LORD.

Leading proofs of personal virtue

I. A strong desire for knowledge of the real state of character. "Judge me, O Lord." Implying—

1. No self-confidence, no self-deception.

2. A desire to be made right, whatever cost.

II. A recognition of God's mercy. "For Thy loving kindness is before mine eyes." The life of the godly is marked by—

1. The strongest love and gratitude.

2. The highest courage.

III. A consciousness of sincere conduct. "I have walked in Thy truth." The godly man hates hypocrisy and loathes shame.

IV. A strong repugnance to all ungodly society, "I have not sat with vain persons,"

1. He declares that he never had any fellowship with them  -- v.4

2. He expresses his hatred of them –-v.5

3. He prays that he may not be gathered to their company -- v.9

V. A delight in the public worship of God. "I will wash mine hands in innocency,"  True worship—

1. Requires personal preparation.

2. Consists in public worship.

3. Is inspired with the presence of God.

VI. A fixed determination to walk ever with the holy. "But as for me, I will walk,"  --v. 11-12

The character of an upright man sketched by himself

To do this requires much self-examination. Yet there may he circumstances when such work becomes necessary.

I. The Psalmist had a good foundation on which his life was built.

1. Trust in Jehovah (verse 1).

2. God's loving kindness (verse 5).

3. God's truth; that is, His faithfulness (verse 3).

All the supports of his integrity were outside himself. Happy is the man that can stay his mind on Divine faithfulness and love! If these cease to sustain, moral and spiritual worth decline from motive and hope.

II. The life built on this foundation is worthy of examination. It was a life of—

1. Integrity (verse 11).

2. Straightforward progress (verse 1).

3. Avoiding evil associations (verses 4, 5).

4. Building of true character, Cultivation of holy worship, song, and thanksgiving are absolutely needed (verses 6-8, 12).

Pr 26:23 ¶ Burning lips and a wicked heart are like a potsherd covered with silver dross.

 

Burning lips and a wicked heart

 

Splendid, shining, smooth lips;

Burning lips which make great professions of friendship

but are like a vessel plated over with base metal to make it resemble

silver;

but it is only a vile pot, and even the outside is not pure.

 

Burning lips; lips that make warm professions of love.

 

Silver dross; glittering, yet worthless.

 

Warm professions can no more give value to insincerity than silver dross to stone pottery.

 

Burning lips-With malice or hatred: A slanderous or evil tongue.

 

Silver Dross-Such a tongue and heart are of no real worth, and is proven in heat, as dross does of silver.

 

Burning lips --- furious, passionate, outrageous words, burning in malice, and persecuting those to whom, or of whom, they are spoken;

ill words and ill-will agree as well together as a potsherd and the dross of silver, which, now that the pot is broken and the dross separated from the silver, are fit to be thrown together to the dunghill or potters field

 

a wicked heart disguising itself with burning lips,

burning with the professions of love and friendship,

and even persecuting a man with flatteries;

this is like a potsherd covered with the scum or dross of silver, with which one that is weak may be imposed upon, as if it were of some value, but a wise man is soon aware of the cheat.

 

There is a film of fair speech like a coating of silver, but underneath is deceit.  They appear to glow with love, but in very truth malice is smoldering in their souls.  Lord, save us from lying lips and malicious hearts.
