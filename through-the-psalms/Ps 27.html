Pr 27:22 ¶ Though thou shouldest bray a fool in a mortar among wheat with a pestle, yet will not his foolishness depart from him.

An obstinate, self-willed, opinionated, and unprincipled man cannot be corrected. A leopard's spots cannot be changed and a fool will remain a fool, as Solomon well knew from bitter personal experience.

One of the greatest fools mentioned in Scripture is Esau, Jacob's twin brother.

Esau was born into a family with every earthly and heavenly advantage.

God had sovereignly decreed that Jacob would receive the birthright and the blessing, but Esau was still left with enormous advantages over the rest of mankind. His father was Isaac. His grandfather was Abraham.

Heb 11:10 For he looked for a city which hath foundations, whose builder and maker is God.

But Esau lacked spiritual vision and went by vain imaginations. He had no eye for the unseen, the spiritual, and the eternal. He had no interest in those "exceeding great and precious promises" that God had given to Abraham and confirmed to Isaac and his seed

Though Esau was born with so many advantages he became a fool. He was born in the home of believers, but failed to benefit from that advantage.

Esau quickly developed a love for this world. He became a skilled hunter. He had an eye like a hawk, he could move through tall grass like a leopard, and his arrow never missed. He was bold, brave, rugged, hairy, and sunburned, but he was a heathen. A little boy in a big body; full body and empty soul... a profane person, who failed of the grace of God."

Esau's preoccupation with this world blinded him to the world to come. So he sold his birthright, which was never his to sell, then wept bitter tears when Jacob stole what really was already his: the undiluted blessing of God. The pestle and mortar were busy enough that day.

Ge 27:34 And when Esau heard the words of his father, he cried with a great and exceeding bitter cry, and said unto his father, Bless me, even me also, O my father.

Esau "cried with a great and exceeding bitter cry," But Esau was a fool and soon forgot and wandered away from what he had been taught.

 9 Then went Esau unto Ishmael, and took unto the wives which he had Mahalath the daughter of Ishmael Abraham's son, the sister of Nebajoth, to be his wife.

 

The thought never occurred to Esau that Ishmael was as pagan as the Canaanites and that a marriage to a daughter of Ishmael would no more please his parents than his marriages to Judith and Bashemath.

Esau bdid this with a sincere desire to obey and please his parents.  Having heard the pious advice which Isaac gave to Jacob, he therefore went and took a wife from the family of his grandfather Abraham, as Jacob was desired to do out of the family of his maternal uncle Laban.  Mahalath, whom he took to wife, stood in the same degree of relationship to Isaac his father as Rachel did to his mother Rebekah.  Esau married his father's niece; Jacob married his mother's niece.  It was obviously to please his parents that Esau took this additional wife.  It is supposed that Ishmael must have been dead thirteen or fourteen years before this time, and that going to Ishmael signifies only going to the family of Ishmael.  

Isaac was now about one hundred and thirty-six or one hundred and thirty-seven years of age, and Jacob seventy-seven, and as Ishmael died in the one hundred and thirty-seventh year of his age, which according to the scripture was the one hundred and twenty-third of Isaac, then Ishmael must have been dead about fourteen years.

After that there was no more pestle and mortar. The fool was left to his folly. But other fools might have thought that Esau was a sensible fellow. After all, he got on well in this world. He settled in mount Seir and became the patriarch of the formidable Edomite race. One of his grandsons, Amalek, founded another nation that was bitterly hostile to Israel. And the Edomite race spawned Herod the Great, the infamous ruler who massacred the babes of Bethlehem in an attempt to murder the infant Christ of God.
