Pr 29:6 ¶ In the transgression of an evil man there is a snare: but the righteous doth sing and rejoice.

The righteous are joyful because they are free of sin.                                                              The wicked are trapped and enslaved by their own sin.

The subject of traps continues. Sin captures and destroys us when we stray into its territory. A snare (moqesh) is a noose-like trap. Some hunters used it like a lasso and roped the heads or noses of their prey. Bird hunters set it on the ground, then disguised and baited it. When the bird stepped into the snare, it snapped and tightened on its leg. Sin, like the flatterer, is a smooth and cunning stalker. Like hungry animals, we sinful humans unwittingly fall into its clutches while trying to satisfy our carnal appetites.

Evil people wander in the woods of wickedness but they will inevitably be caught in the snare of sin. Righteous people, live without this, When we adhere to God’s holy path, we have no secrets. No hidden sins lurk to find us out and trap us (Nu.32:23). We walk through life with a song in our hearts and on our lips because we are truly free (Ps.119:45).

Pr 29:7 ¶ The righteous considereth the cause of the poor: but the wicked regardeth not to know it.

The righteous champion justice for the poor. The wicked do not care about justice for the poor.

The poor are needy in circumstances/disadvantages beyond their control

Pr 29:8 ¶ Scornful men bring a city into a snare: but wise men turn away wrath.

The foolish mock restrictions and stir up a city. The wise turn away anger and calm a city.

Pr 29:9 ¶ If a wise man contendeth with a foolish man, whether he rage or laugh, there is no rest.

The wise avoid court cases with fools because they are unreasonable.

Pr 29:10 ¶ The bloodthirsty hate the upright: but the just seek his soul.

Our attitudes reveal whether we are righteous/wise or wicked/foolish. These proverbs specifically mention areas we should give attention to,

  • accepting rebuke and correction (v. 1)
  • upholding righteousness (v. 2)
  • respecting and honoring our parents (v. 3)
  • guarding against sexual immorality (v. 3)
  • avoiding greed and covetousness (v. 4)
  • having no part of flattery or deceitfulness (v. 5)
  • being alert to sin and its dangers (v. 6)
  • caring about the plight of the poor (v. 7)
  • turning away from strife and dissension (vv. 8-9)
  • protecting and supporting people with integrity (v. 10)

“A man is not what he thinks he is, but what he thinks, he is.” Our behavior is determined by our attitudes. It is easy to be self-deceived (Jas.1:22). Far too many are not what they pretend to be.

We must constantly examine our attitudes                                                                                                                              We must truly judge what we really are. We must ask ourselves,

“Do my thoughts and my way of thinking agree with God’s Word?” Our sinful nature is subject to stray from God’s path; we must regularly pray as David prayed:                                                                                                                                 Ps 139:23 Search me, O God, and know my heart: try me, and know my thoughts:                                                                                                                              24 And see if there be any wicked way in me, and lead me in the way everlasting.

We cannot trust our hearts. We should never be steered by our own logic or reasoning but by the principles of God’s Holy Word. Never take counsel from your flesh. Neither be easily swayed by the opinions of others, by those who are deemed wise by the world. Remember the words of the Lord through the prophet Jeremiah:

“The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately wicked: who can know it? I the Lord search the heart, I try the reins, even to give every man according to his ways, and according to the fruit of his doings” (Je.17:9-10).

 

The wise always do what is right and leave the results up to God.

 

2. (29:11-24) Their conduct at home and in society.

Successful nations are made up of leaders and citizens who recognize that how they carry out their duties has a tremendous impact on those around them. This is true in government, in business, and in the home. All segments of society will be strengthened by people who follow the practical wisdom of these proverbs.

V. 11 The wise hold back their thoughts and feelings. The foolish vent their feelings with others.

If we are wise, we will control our emotions and our words. Mind (ruach), usually translated as spirit, refers here to all that is in our spirit—everything we think and feel. It is foolish to unleash our feelings, thoughts, and opinions all at once and to lose our tempers. Instead, we should guard against speaking out too freely and learn to keep silent in the heat of the moment. We should never act or speak when emotionally upset or provoked, but rather wait until our feelings have settled down. Remember, some opinions are better kept to ourselves.

“Let all bitterness, and wrath, and anger, and clamour, and evil speaking, be put away from you, with all malice” (Ep.4:31).

“Put them in mind to be subject to principalities and powers, to obey magistrates, to be ready to every good work, To speak evil of no man, to be no brawlers, but gentle, showing all meekness unto all men” (Tit.3:1-2).

“A prudent man concealeth knowledge: but the heart of fools proclaimeth foolishness” (Pr.12:23).

V. 12 Unwise rulers listen to lies and prompt others to sin.

Discerning between truthful and untruthful information is a vital skill for leaders. The righteous embrace the truth, and they take a stand against all lies and liars (20:28). If we are in a position of responsibility, we must remember to verify all information and learn to recognize the traits of liars. We must take strong, swift action against any who are less than completely honest in the discharge of their duties. If those who serve under us discover that we are weak or lenient in this area, they will pounce on our failure. They themselves will begin to lie and become deceitful. Like David, we must set an impeccably high standard of integrity for those who work under us:

“Mine eyes shall be upon the faithful of the land, that they may dwell with me: he that walketh in a perfect way, he shall serve me. He that worketh deceit shall not dwell within my house: he that telleth lies shall not tarry in my sight” (Ps.101:6-7).

“Lying lips are abomination to the Lord: but they that deal truly are his delight” (Pr.12:22).

“Take away the wicked from before the king, and his throne shall be established in righteousness” (Pr.25:5).

V. 13 The wicked oppress the poor and refuse to acknowledge their value to God.

God is no respecter of persons. Our financial standing is insignificant to Him. Tragically, many people look down on the poor and consider themselves superior to or better than them. This sinful, prideful attitude is most displeasing to God. It can lead to the mistreatment of the poor and needy as well as to taking advantage of them.

Socially and financially, poor people and their persecutors may have little in common, but their paths cross at one point: both have been given light by the Lord. What is this light?

⇒The light of physical life (see outline and notes—20:27 for more discussion). God made them both. Both are accountable to Him.

“So God created man in his own image, in the image of God created he him; male and female created he them” (Ge.1:27).

⇒The light of spiritual illumination: the ability to recognize, trust, worship, and serve God.

“For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who hold the truth in unrighteousness; Because that which may be known of God is manifest in them; for God hath showed it unto them. For the invisible things of him from the creation of the world are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made, even his eternal power and Godhead; so that they are without excuse” (Ro.1:18-20).

“But if our gospel be hid, it is hid to them that are lost: In whom the god of this world hath blinded the minds of them which believe not, lest the light of the glorious gospel of Christ, who is the image of God, should shine unto them” (2 Co.4:3-4).

“For ye were sometimes darkness, but now are ye light in the Lord: walk as children of light” (Ep.5:8).

⇒The light of the Lord Jesus Christ, the Savior of all people:

“The people that walked in darkness have seen a great light: they that dwell in the land of the shadow of death, upon them hath the light shined” (Is.9:2).

“In him was life; and the life was the light of men. And the light shineth in darkness; and the darkness comprehended it not” (Jn.1:4-5).

“Then spake Jesus again unto them, saying, I am the light of the world: he that followeth me shall not walk in darkness, but shall have the light of life” (Jn.8:12).

We who are blessed with the ability and opportunity to earn a sufficient living must never look down on those who are less fortunate. We must view them with compassion and extend Christ’s love to them. Many are poor because of circumstances beyond their control. Others are destitute because of their own choices and destructive actions. Regardless, we must always remember their value to God. They stand on equal ground with everyone else in His sight. God made us all: we exist because of the wonderful works of His hands (Ps.139:14). He loves us and gave His Son to die for each and every one of us. He wants us all to know the life that is found in Him.

“For the love of Christ constraineth us; because we thus judge, that if one died for all, then were all dead: And that he died for all, that they which live should not henceforth live unto themselves, but unto him which died for them, and rose again” (2 Co.5:14-15).

“The rich and poor meet together: the Lord is the maker of them all” (Pr.22:2).

“How much less to him that accepteth not the persons of princes, nor regardeth the rich more than the poor? for they all are the work of his hands” (Jb.34:19).

V. 14 Righteous leaders secure their throne or administration because of their fairness.

A righteous king does not abuse the poor. He executes justice fairly, according to the truth. He does not discriminate against the poor and he does not side with their greedy, wealthy oppressors (v. 4). As a result, his throne usually becomes secure and he reigns a long time. The reason: the integrity of just rulers assures God’s favor upon a nation and brings stability to a society. In addition, the ruler is appreciated by the vast majority of people, which means they seek to protect and defend him (24:23-25).

As responsible citizens, we, too, should recognize the value of leaders who are faithful to their duties and fair with everyone, the poor and weak as well as the rich and powerful. We should support and keep these leaders in office as long as possible.

“Mercy and truth preserve the king: and his throne is upholden by mercy” (Pr.20:28).

“But with righteousness shall he judge the poor, and reprove with equity for the meek of the earth: and he shall smite the earth with the rod of his mouth, and with the breath of his lips shall he slay the wicked” (Is.11:4).

V. 15 The wise teach their children through correction and discipline. The foolish allow their children to do as they wish.

No matter what the latest child-rearing philosophy or fad is, children arrive in this world with a human nature. They inherit a nature that will, at times, behave selfishly, rebel, become angry, and commit a host of other mischievous and sometimes harmful acts (22:15). Of course, the older the child, the more serious the behavior. Only discipline can unbind children from their foolishness and make room in their hearts for wisdom. The rod refers to physical punishment or spanking. Reproof is verbal correction. It is not enough to instruct our children in wisdom and only verbally correct them when they err. Our teaching must be accompanied by physical discipline. Children do not have an innate desire to do right; therefore, they must learn that disobedient and sinful behavior results in punishment. If we spare the rod, we will experience the stabbing pain and humiliation of rearing unwise and foolish children. A wise parent will use mild spanking as a tool to teach children to avoid disobedient (lawless), sinful, and harmful behavior. This is the unmistakable teaching of God Himself.

“He that spareth his rod hateth his son: but he that loveth him chasteneth him betimes” (Pr.13:24).

“Foolishness is bound in the heart of a child; but the rod of correction shall drive it far from him” (Pr.22:15).

“For whom the Lord loveth he chasteneth, and scourgeth every son whom he receiveth” (He.12:6).

V. 16 The wicked cause the spread of sin, but the righteous will witness their downfall.

The prosperity of the wicked has vexed God’s people for generations (Ps.2:1; Je.12:1). In a world (community, society, or nation) where Satan reigns, sin runs rampant and wicked people thrive. Sometimes, we live to see the wicked’s demise, but often we do not. However, we should never despair, for God’s Word repeatedly casts our eyes to the promise of a future day when all enemies will be conquered (Ps.37:34; 91:8). This day will come at the return of the Lord Jesus Christ to earth (Re.19:11-21). On that day, this proverb will be fulfilled.

“Wait on the Lord, and keep his way, and he shall exalt thee to inherit the land: when the wicked are cut off, thou shalt see it” (Ps.37:34).

“Only with thine eyes shalt thou behold and see the reward of the wicked” (Ps.91:8).

“Teaching us that, denying ungodliness and worldly lusts, we should live soberly, righteously, and godly, in this present world; Looking for that blessed hope, and the glorious appearing of the great God and our Saviour Jesus Christ” (Tit.2:12-13).

“And the remnant were slain with the sword of him that sat upon the horse, which sword proceeded out of his mouth: and all the fowls were filled with their flesh” (Re.19:21).

V. 17 Diligent parents know peace and joy because they discipline their children.

The importance of correcting children is reemphasized again, one final time. If we are wise parents, we will chasten our children. We will lovingly, but firmly, fulfill our sacred duty to the Lord by directing our children to the path of life. Without our diligent and consistent help, our sons and daughters will not become wise, at least not under our supervision. Therefore, we must steer our children along the path of wisdom and righteousness. If we do, they will bring us rest and bring delight to our souls:

⇒Rest—a settling peace of heart and mind; freedom from worrying about how our children will behave or what will become of them.

⇒Delight to our souls—delight (ma‘adan) is used of the luxurious delicacies enjoyed only by kings (Ge.49:20). However, many of the world’s most powerful rulers never taste (experience) the exquisite pleasure of a wise child. Solomon never knew this delight. But, we will if we lovingly and consistently correct and discipline our children when they do wrong.

“Chasten thy son while there is hope, and let not thy soul spare for his crying” (Pr.19:18).

“I have no greater joy than to hear that my children walk in truth” (3 Jn.1:4).

V. 18 The righteous are happy because they keep God’s law. The wicked reject God’s revelation and perish.

God’s Word is a powerful force that preserves, protects, and keeps us on the path of righteousness. Commentators
differ as to the meaning of vision, but the second statement of the proverb defines it. Law is parallel to vision and establishes that the subject of the proverb is the Word of God. Vision is divine revelation given through the prophets:

“And the child Samuel ministered unto the Lord before Eli. And the word of the Lord was precious in those days; there was no open vision” (1 Sa.3:1).

“The vision of Isaiah the son of Amoz, which he saw concerning Judah and Jerusalem in the days of Uzziah, Jotham, Ahaz, and Hezekiah, kings of Judah” (Is.1:1).

“I have also spoken by the prophets, and I have multiplied visions, and used similitudes, by the ministry of the prophets” (Hos.12:10).

“And the Lord answered me, and said, Write the vision, and make it plain upon tables, that he may run that readeth it. For the vision is yet for an appointed time, but at the end it shall speak, and not lie: though it tarry, wait for it; because it will surely come, it will not tarry” (Hab.2:2-3).

“The law, the prophets and the wisdom literature meet in this verse.” We need to fill our minds, hearts, and lives with the Word of God. This is why it is important to read the Bible daily and to consistently sit under its preaching and teaching. God’s Word guides us along the right path and protects us from straying into sin. Furthermore, God’s Word is a cleansing agent in our lives:

“Now ye are clean through the word which I have spoken unto you” (Jn.15:3).

“That he might sanctify and cleanse it with the washing of water by the word” (Ep.5:26).

When we do not have the constant influence of God’s Word in our lives, we become unrestrained. Perish means to loosen or cast off restraint. When we ignore, neglect, or deny God’s Word, we eventually loose ourselves from the boundaries set by God Himself. We “run wild” (NLT). As a result, we are likely to fall into sin and ultimately perish (Jas.1:15). Grasp the correct meaning of this word picture: it does not present the Bible as a rope that binds us or a prison that confines us. The Bible is a hedge that protects us from the dangers that lurk outside its boundaries. If we keep (shamar) God’s Word—hedge our lives with it—we will be happy. We will walk about freely inside its borders and remain unharmed by the dangers of sin.

“But whoso looketh into the perfect law of liberty, and continueth therein, he being not a forgetful hearer, but a doer of the work, this man shall be blessed in his deed” (Jas.1:25).

“And I will walk at liberty: for I seek thy precepts” (Ps.119:45).

“Behold, the days come, saith the Lord GOD, that I will send a famine in the land, not a famine of bread, nor a thirst for water, but of hearing the words of the Lord: And they shall wander from sea to sea, and from the north even to the east, they shall run to and fro to seek the word of the Lord, and shall not find it” (Am.8:11-12).

V. 19 Wise employers correct and teach their workers.

Children are not the only ones who need correction: sometimes employees (servants) do as well. Verbal correction and instruction are not always enough to produce right conduct. We all have selfish and sinful natures: we are far short of what we should be. Therefore, many tend to push the limits as far and as long as allowed. When this happens in the work place, stern measures are sometimes needed. In ancient history, unfaithful employees were often scourged with the rod, but Jewish law placed strict limits on this mode of punishment (Ex.21:20, 26-27). In modern times, most societies use other methods to modify employee behavior and to motivate workers to productivity.

The lesson is clear: although employees may understand what is expected of them, if there is no penalty for poor work, many will not perform their duties—not well—not diligently nor efficiently. Therefore, a wise society or
employer will establish clear, unmistakable guidelines for all workers. And they will have a clear understanding about the consequences of poor performances. They will maintain the right and establish the authority to hold workers accountable—to correct or release them if needed.

“And whatsoever ye do in word or deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus, giving thanks to God and the Father by him….Fathers, provoke not your children to anger, lest they be discouraged” (Col.3:17, 21).

“And that ye study to be quiet, and to do your own business, and to work with your own hands, as we commanded you; That ye may walk honestly toward them that are without, and that ye may have lack of nothing” (1 Th.4:11-12).

“For even when we were with you, this we commanded you, that if any would not work, neither should he eat” (2 Th.3:10).

V. 20 Speaking hastily is worse than foolishness.

Controlling speech is one of the primary keys to skillful, successful living. It is mentioned frequently throughout Proverbs. The present proverb climaxes the teaching on this subject: we have no hope of success in life if we do not learn to bridle our tongues.

Fools are presented throughout Proverbs as being unteachable, unreprovable, and beyond help. But there is more hope for fools than for people who speak quickly and without thinking—who cannot keep their mouths shut. This statement is also made of those who are wise in their own eyes (26:12).

“Wherefore, my beloved brethren, let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath” (Jas.1:19).

“But I say unto you, That every idle word that men shall speak, they shall give account thereof in the day of judgment. For by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned” (Mt.12:36-37).

V. 21 Foolish employers pamper and fail to correct workers.

Pampering a servant from childhood or an employee from the moment he or she is hired will cause problems later. What it will bring in the end is unclear because the Hebrew word for son (manown) appears only here in the Old
Testament. Various Bible versions translate the word differently, but son is the best translation. It suggests that the servant will not learn to work and that the master will have to provide for him throughout his life. It may also mean that the servant or employee assumes he or she will receive something that is not due him or her, perhaps an advance in position or an inheritance. The lesson is clear. When an employee is favored or pampered in the workplace, it
causes…

  • bad feelings and strife
  • jealousy and envy
  • gossip and rumors
  • murmuring and grumbling
  • inefficiency and less production
  • higher cost and less profit
  • a diminished image of company or product excellence

In addition, a pampered employee often begins to expect more and more of something that is not rightly his/hers: more authority, higher pay, more days off, longer vacations, and a host of other perks or favors. The Lord warns the employers and managers of the world: be wise, not foolish. It is most unwise to pamper workers, especially when they are first hired.

At the same time, other Scripture teaches that righteous employers care about their employees (see outline and notes—Ep.6:5-9, for more discussion). They value dedicated and productive workers who give their best efforts and make sacrifices for the business. They are fair and considerate of their needs. When the cost of living increases, they increase their wages accordingly. When an employee goes beyond and does more than what is required, wise employers show their appreciation and reward him or her accordingly. Upright employers never try to take advantage of an employee by getting as much work as possible from the individual for as little money as possible.

Righteous, wise employers and employees show mutual respect and appreciation for each other. When each does his or her best for the other, the result will be a long, satisfying, productive, and mutually profitable relationship.

“Not slothful in business; fervent in spirit; serving the Lord” (Ro.12:11).

“Servants [employees], be obedient to them that are your masters [employers] according to the flesh, with fear and trembling, in singleness of your heart, as unto Christ; Not with eyeservice, as menpleasers; but as the servants of Christ, doing the will of God from the heart; With good will doing service, as to the Lord, and not to men: Knowing that whatsoever good thing any man doeth, the same shall he receive of the Lord, whether he be bond or free. And, ye masters, do the same things unto them, forbearing threatening: knowing that your Master also is in heaven; neither is there respect of persons with him” (Ep.6:5-9).

“For the scripture saith, Thou shalt not muzzle the ox that treadeth out the corn. And, The labourer is worthy of his reward” (1 Ti.5:18).

“Thou shalt not oppress an hired servant that is poor and needy, whether he be of thy brethren, or of thy strangers that are in thy land within thy gates: At his day thou shalt give him his hire, neither shall the sun go down upon it; for he is poor, and setteth his heart upon it: lest he cry against thee unto the Lord, and it be sin unto thee” (De.24:14-15).

“Go to the ant, thou sluggard; consider her ways, and be wise” (Pr.6:6).

“Wealth gotten by vanity shall be diminished: but he that gathereth by labour shall increase” (Pr.13:11).

“He also that is slothful in his work is brother to him that is a great waster” (Pr.18:9).

V. 22 The foolish stir up strife and commit all kinds of sins in anger.

Note a final admonition about anger: avoid people whose tempers are uncontrolled. They are troublemakers and constantly stir up strife. When people are inflamed with anger, they commit all kinds of sins and cause damage that still remains after their fury cools.

No matter how disturbing the situation, we must strive to control our tempers. Harsh, cutting words cannot be taken back. Damage done in the heat of a moment cannot be undone. The Bible warns us not to sin in the heat of anger (Ep.4:26). Self-control is the key to victory over a quick temper. God’s indwelling Spirit produces this fruit in our lives (Ga.5:23).

“A wrathful man stirreth up strife: but he that is slow to anger appeaseth strife” (Pr.15:18).

“Make no friendship with an angry man; and with a furious man thou shalt not go” (Pr.22:24).

“Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the sun go down upon your wrath” (Ep.4:26).

V. 23 The righteous are honored for their humility. The wicked are proud and will be abased.

Human logic reasons that the way to success is through self-elevation, but this is a false conclusion. Scripture clearly teaches that pride and self-promotion do not exalt people. Instead, they eventually result in dishonor and humiliation. God’s Word declares that the arrogant will be humbled through a painful, disgraceful fall. While those who maintain a humble spirit will be held in high esteem—if not in this life, then in the next

God Himself will exalt us throughout eternity if we humbly bow before Him and submit to Him (Jas.4:10). The great example of this is the Lord Jesus Christ, who was meek and lowly in heart; yet He is acknowledged by most people to be the greatest man of human history (Mt.11:29). As believers, we are to walk humbly on this earth.

“Let this mind be in you, which was also in Christ Jesus: Who, being in the form of God, thought it not robbery to be equal with God: But made himself of no reputation, and took upon him the form of a servant, and was made in the likeness of men: And being found in fashion as a man, he humbled himself, and became obedient unto death, even the death of the cross. Wherefore God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name: That at the name of Jesus every knee should bow, of things in heaven, and things in earth, and things under the earth; And that every tongue should confess that Jesus Christ is Lord, to the glory of God the Father” (Ph.2:5-11).

“For whosoever exalteth himself shall be abased; and he that humbleth himself shall be exalted” (Lu.14:11).

“Humble yourselves in the sight of the Lord, and he shall lift you up” (Jas.4:10).

“Pride goeth before destruction, and an haughty spirit before a fall. Better it is to be of an humble spirit with the lowly, than to divide the spoil with the proud” (Pr.16:18-19).

V. 24 The wicked assist thieves and condemn themselves: they have to keep silent.

Jewish law stated that a person who withheld information about a crime shared in the guilt of the crime (Le.5:1). In God’s eyes, if you aid a thief you bring double judgment upon yourself. You are guilty of the theft itself and of failing to provide honest testimony about it. Partner or accomplice (chalaq) is usually translated as divide in the Old Testament. It means that the accomplice received a share of the stolen goods for his involvement in the crime. If we have integrity, the prospect of gain will not influence us to do something that is wrong or questionable. This warning expands on the very first warning given in Proverbs:

“My son, if sinners entice thee, consent thou not. If they say, Come with us, let us lay wait for blood, let us lurk privily for the innocent without cause: Let us swallow them up alive as the grave; and whole, as those that go down into the pit: We shall find all precious substance, we shall fill our houses with spoil: Cast in thy lot among us; let us all have one purse: My son, walk not thou in the way with them; refrain thy foot from their path: For their feet run to evil, and make haste to shed blood” (Pr.1:10-16).

“Now we command you, brethren, in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that ye withdraw yourselves from every brother that walketh disorderly, and not after the tradition which he received of us” (2 Th.3:6).

“Neither be partaker of other men's sins: keep thyself pure” (1 Ti.5:22).

“And if a soul sin, and hear the voice of swearing, and is a witness, whether he hath seen or known of it; if he do not utter it, then he shall bear his iniquity” (Le.5:1).

Thought 1. God’s Word is certainly not silent concerning what behavior is acceptable in the world and what is not. In this particular section, Solomon points out several key areas we should monitor closely in our lives:

⇒we need to control our tempers and our tongues at all times (vv. 11, 20, 22).

⇒we must refuse to associate with sinful people and criminals (v. 24).

⇒we need to humble ourselves before the Lord and seek to serve rather than be served (v. 23).

⇒we need to saturate ourselves with God’s Word. It will keep us on the right path and help us to guard against sinful, unwise behavior (v. 18).

⇒we need to be loving and consistent in disciplining our children. We have to be faithful in fulfilling our often painful duty of chastening them in order that they will be wise. This is crucial to the continuing success of our society. One undisciplined generation can destroy a nation (vv. 15, 17).

⇒we need to choose leaders (when possible) who maintain a high standard of integrity, leaders who have nothing to do with dishonesty and dishonest people. We need to support officials who cannot be influenced by money or promises of gain, and insist that they be fair and merciful to the poor (vv. 12-14).

⇒we need to be firm and fair with those who work under us. We cannot allow emotional attachment to employees to cause us to be lenient toward them. We have to understand that we harm ourselves, our employees, and ultimately our nation when we fail to hold the workforce to a high standard of performance (vv. 19, 21).

3. (29:25-27) Their desire for security and justice.

If we are wise, we have an unwavering faith in the Lord. We do not fret when threatened by evildoers, and we are not desperate when wrong prevails. Instead, we trust in the Lord and believe what He says and promises. In doing so, we develop a strong faith that enables us to rest in Him and to wait for Him to keep His Word.

V. 25 The righteous trust the Lord and are protected by Him. The wicked are trapped by their fear of men.

If we have trusted in the Lord, we will live righteously—do the right thing regardless of the consequences. We will not be afraid of what people may think or do because of our stand for what is right. Fear (acharadah) means to tremble with terror. We fall into a dangerous trap if we allow a fear of other people to possess us. Fear will compel us to lie, keep silent, bow down, give in, or compromise to avoid any unpleasant repercussions or retribution. Abraham lied and betrayed his wife because he was afraid of Pharoah and Abimelech (Ge.12, 20). Peter denied Christ because he was afraid of what people might do to him (Mt.26:69-75).

Jesus has warned us: if we are truly faithful to Him and His Word, we will be persecuted (Jn.15:18-21). It is impossible to live for Christ and avoid persecution (2 Ti.3:12). Being faithful to God’s Word and living righteously may cost us a promotion or even our jobs. It may even affect our relationships with friends and family. In some places persecution is as mild as being mocked or ridiculed; whereas, in other countries, loyalty to Christ and God’s Word may cost us our freedom or even our lives. Someplace, every day, faithful believers are slain for the cause of Christ.

If you truly trust in the Lord, you will not cower at the feet of men. Christ will strengthen you, enabling you to stand boldly. When threatened, just call upon Him and do not retreat. Stand firm; refuse to be intimidated by others. Be assured that you are in God’s hands and that He is in control of your life. Know that He is all-powerful and that He is greater than your enemies. Your security is in His providential care. Even if He allows you to suffer loss or harm, maintain your trust in Him (Jb.13:15).

V. 26 The righteous trust the Lord for justice. The wicked seek the favor of rulers.

When we trust the Lord, we do not fret when justice is not served in human courts. Confidence in the Lord frees us from the driving compulsion for vengeance. Why? Because we believe what God says: He is the ultimate and final judge. Faith releases us to rest in His Word and power. It enables us to suffer loss with peace because we know that God will do what is right. A day is coming when He will correct all the wrongs done on earth and execute perfect justice. However, there are many who place their trust in government officials for justice. But as we all know, human government cannot bring perfect justice to earth. When we trust the Lord, though, we can rest assured that He will balance all the scales in His time.

“Dearly beloved, avenge not yourselves, but rather give place unto wrath: for it is written, Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord” (Ro.12:19).

V. 27 The righteous detest the lifestyle of the wicked, and the wicked detest the righteous.

When we walk in God’s righteousness, we refuse to compromise with evil and deceitful people. As a result, the wicked often despise us. Note the use of the term abomination, a term that provokes the strongest negative emotion. When we stand against a person who promotes wickedness, we must be alert to opposition. The wicked may bitterly and angrily attack us.

    Pr 29:1 ¶ He, that being often reproved hardeneth his neck, shall suddenly be destroyed, and that without remedy.

    First, sudden destruction without remedy

    Second, the sons and daughters of God—we who have a relationship with God through faith in Jesus Christ—may suffer premature death if we persist in sin. God does not ignore the sins of His children. He reproves us when we disobey Him. Our Heavenly Father is longsuffering and patient with us, and He offers many rebukes—many opportunities for repentance. But the Lord will not allow us to willfully continue in sin. He will never simply accept it or overlook it.

    If we are stiff-necked and hardhearted toward our sin, we may eventually cross a line where God’s patience is exhausted. At this point, God may take us suddenly—instantly, unexpectedly—Because of our persistence in sin, He may choose to allow our earthly life to end as the final step of chastisement. The fact that this is an act of judgment cannot be ignored, but it is also an act of love. Note the teaching of the New Testament concerning this final step of chastisement:

    “To deliver such an one unto Satan for the destruction of the flesh, that the spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord Jesus” (1 Co.5:5).

    “For this cause many are weak and sickly among you, and many sleep. For if we would judge ourselves, we should not be judged. But when we are judged, we are chastened of the Lord, that we should not be condemned with the world” (1 Co.11:30-32).

    Sometimes God may allow the natural consequences of sin to take their course in the lives of the hardhearted, resulting in death. Sometimes God may remove His hand of protection and allow some sickness, accident, or tragedy to claim our lives. Sometimes He may seem to intervene directly, as with Ananias and Sapphira who were mysteriously stricken dead because of their sin (Ac.5:1-11). The teaching of Scripture is clear: God will not allow us—His genuine sons and daughters—to continue in sin (See outline and notes—Ac.5:5-6; 1 Co.5:3-5; 1 Co.11:30 for more discussion. See also Deeper Study #1—1 Jn.5:16 for more discussion).

    Third, God may suddenly destroy nations where sin runs rampant. Hezekiah’s men surely selected this proverb as a national warning to backslidden Judah. God had repeatedly rebuked the sinful nation through the messages of the prophets, but they ignored their warnings and continued without repenting. Their destruction would suddenly come, and it did at the hand of Nebuchadnezzar approximately one hundred years later.

    Prov.29:8

    The words of this proverb picture either the kindling or quenching of a fire. Bring into a snare (not the best translation) or stir up (puach) literally means to breathe or blow air or fan. Scorners or mockers—people who scoff at righ-teousness—are troublemakers. They fan the embers of a heated situation and start a fire of strife that spreads throughout the city (26:21). Wise people smother the coals by seeking to calm the situation before it bursts out and causes great damage. The wise are peacemakers.

    “Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God” (Mt.5:9)

    “Let us therefore follow after the things which make for peace, and things wherewith one may edify another” (Ro.14:19).

    “Deceit is in the heart of them that imagine evil: but to the counsellors of peace is joy” (Pr.12:20).

    “As coals are to burning coals, and wood to fire; so is a contentious man to kindle strife” (Pr.26:21).

    29:9

     

    Wise people know there is no point in trying to resolve a conflict with fools. Fools are unreasonable: they are unwilling to compromise or to acknowledge they are wrong. In fact, they will never even consider a different perspective from their own and will refuse to reach any peaceable agreement.

    Note how fools respond to matters serious enough to require legal action: they either rage (ragaz), becoming intensely angry to the point of shaking with emotion, or they make light of the situation by laughing, jeering, or mocking. It is useless to seek negotiation or compromise with them. They will never put a matter to rest.

    “And the servant of the Lord must not strive; but be gentle unto all men, apt to teach, patient” (2 Ti.2:24).

    “Let a bear robbed of her whelps meet a man, rather than a fool in his folly” (Pr.17:12).

    “Go not forth hastily to strive, lest thou know not what to do in the end thereof, when thy neighbour hath put thee to shame” (Pr.25:8).

     

    29:10

     

    The righteous care about the upright, those who are truly honest. The wicked are bloodthirsty and hate them.

    Wicked people will resort to violence—assault, even murder—when a person with integrity is a threat to them. Soul (nephesh) in the second line indicates that their very lives are at stake. “Honest people are hated by fools, who would prefer killing the honest so they could not testify against the wicked in court.” We who follow God’s Word must help and protect the righteous when they are in peril.

    “Bear ye one another’s burdens, and so fulfil the law of Christ” (Ga.6:2).

    “As we have therefore opportunity, let us do good unto all men, especially unto them who are of the household of faith” (Ga.6:10).

    “Deliver me from the workers of iniquity, and save me from bloody men” (Ps.59:2).

    “Not as Cain, who was of that wicked one, and slew his brother. And wherefore slew he him? Because his own works were evil, and his brother’s righteous” (1 Jn.3:12).
